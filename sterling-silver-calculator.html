What Is Sterling Silver?

Sterling silver is a metal alloy composed of 92.5% pure silver and 7.5% other metals — almost always copper. It is the most widely used silver standard in the world, forming the basis of virtually all silver jewelry, tableware, trophies, and decorative objects sold today. The "925" stamp is the internationally recognised hallmark that certifies this composition.

Pure silver (999 fine) is too soft for practical use: it bends, scratches and deforms under normal handling. Adding copper transforms it into a metal sturdy enough for daily wear while retaining an exceptionally high silver content and the beautiful white luster that makes silver so desirable.

The History of the 925 Sterling Standard

The sterling silver standard originated in medieval England. The term "sterling" is believed to derive from the Old Norman French word esterlin, meaning "little star" — a reference to the star-shaped marks on early Norman pennies. By the 13th century, England had formally codified 92.5% as the required silver content for coins and silverware.

The Hallmarking Act of 1300 under Edward I established compulsory assay testing — meaning all silver items had to be tested for purity before they could be sold. This legislation, nearly 725 years old, is the foundation of the modern 925 hallmarking system still used worldwide today.

Today, the 925 sterling standard is recognised and legally mandated by governments across Europe, North America, Asia, and Australia, making it the most internationally consistent precious metals specification in existence.

How to Identify Sterling Silver

Genuine 925 sterling silver will carry one or more of the following marks, usually in a small stamped impression that may require a 10× jeweler's loupe to read clearly:

  • 925 — The most universal hallmark; 925 parts per thousand of pure silver
  • STERLING — Common on American silver items from the 19th–20th century
  • STER — Abbreviated form of STERLING, especially on smaller pieces
  • Lion Passant — A walking lion symbol used on British sterling since 1544
  • .925 — Decimal format, common on continental European and modern pieces

Where to look: Inside ring bands, on necklace clasps, on the underside of flatware handles, on the back of brooch pins, and on the underside of hollowware bases.

If an item shows EPNS, EP, A1, or "Silver Plate," it is not solid silver — it is electroplated and has minimal scrap value. See our complete hallmarks guide for a full list of marks.

How This Calculator Works

This calculator uses the formula: Melt Value = Weight (troy oz) × 0.925 × Spot Price. Enter the weight of your item in grams, troy ounces, pennyweights, or kilograms. The calculator automatically converts your weight to troy ounces (1 troy oz = 31.1035 g), multiplies by 92.5% to find the pure silver content, and applies the current COMEX spot price to give you an accurate melt value.

Melt value is the theoretical metal value of the item if refined to pure silver. Dealers typically pay 70–95% of melt value depending on the type of buyer. Online refineries pay the most (90–98%), while pawn shops pay the least (50–70%). Use the Sell or Hold Calculator to model different price scenarios.

Caring for Sterling Silver

Sterling silver tarnishes faster than fine silver because the copper content reacts with hydrogen sulfide in the air, forming silver sulfide — the dark coating you see on untreated pieces. To prevent and remove tarnish:

  • Store in anti-tarnish bags, flannel-lined boxes, or with silica gel packets
  • Keep away from chlorine, bleach, perfume, hairspray, and rubber bands (rubber accelerates tarnish)
  • Polish with a soft silver polishing cloth — avoid abrasive materials that scratch the surface
  • Remove jewelry before swimming, showering, exercising, or doing dishes
  • For silverware: handwash with mild soap; dishwashers can dull the finish over time

Properly stored and cared for, quality 925 sterling silver pieces retain their beauty and silver value for generations.
