What Is Junk Silver?

Junk silver is the common term for pre-1965 US circulated coins containing 90% silver. The "junk" label refers to the absence of numismatic (collectible) value — not the metal value. These coins are highly liquid, easy to verify, and trade close to spot. Most junk silver is sold by face value: a "$1,000 face" bag contains coins with $1,000 in face value and approximately 715–720 troy ounces of pure silver.

Frequently Asked Questions — Junk Silver

Junk silver refers to pre-1965 US coins (dimes, quarters, half dollars, dollars) made of 90% silver with no collectible premium above their metal content. Despite the name, junk silver is highly liquid and widely traded. $1 face value ≈ 0.7234 troy oz of silver.

Multiply the total face value in dollars by 0.7234 to get troy ounces, then multiply by the current spot price. Example: $100 face value × 0.7234 × $—/oz = $—. Or use the face value calculator above.

US dimes and quarters dated 1964 and earlier are 90% silver. Half dollars: 1964 and earlier = 90%; 1965–1970 = 40%; 1971+ = no silver. Dollar coins: Morgan (1878–1921) and Peace (1921–1935) dollars are 90% silver. War nickels (1942–1945 with large mint mark over dome) are 35% silver.

A $1,000 face value bag of 90% silver coins contains approximately 715–720 troy ounces of pure silver. The standard figure used by dealers is 715 troy oz for circulated (worn) coins. At today's spot price that equals $— – $— in melt value.

Junk silver is one of the most practical forms of silver ownership — coins are instantly recognizable, pre-verified for purity, and divisible in small amounts. They trade at low premiums over spot compared to silver rounds or bars. This calculator shows melt value only; actual investment returns depend on entry price and future spot movements.
