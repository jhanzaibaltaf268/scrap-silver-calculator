What Is Junk Silver? "Junk silver" refers to United States coins minted in 1964 or earlier that contain 90% pure silver. Despite the name, there is nothing junky about them — the term simply means the coins carry no numismatic or collectible premium. Dealers, investors, and preppers prize junk silver for its intrinsic precious metal content, government backing, and universal recognizability. Unlike silver bars or rounds, every American can identify a Roosevelt dime or Washington quarter without any expertise. The key event that created the junk silver market was the Coinage Act of 1965. When the US Mint switched to copper-nickel clad coins, the old 90% silver pieces immediately disappeared from circulation — people hoarded them because their metal value exceeded their face value. This is a textbook example of Gresham's Law: "bad money drives out good." Today, pulling a pre-1965 quarter from pocket change is a near-miracle. 💡 The $1.40 Rule — Quick Mental Math Every $1.40 in face value of pre-1965 coins contains approximately one troy ounce of pure silver. So a $10 bag of junk silver holds roughly 7.15 oz of silver. At $35/oz spot, that bag is worth about $250 in melt value alone — regardless of what you paid in face value. Junk Silver Coins: Complete Silver Content Guide Not all junk silver is the same purity. Here is every coin type our calculator covers, with exact silver content per coin: 90% Silver Coins (Pre-1965) Mercury Dime (1916–1945) & Roosevelt Dime (1946–1964) — 0.0723 troy oz pure silver. The most divisible junk silver unit. A $1 face value roll of 10 dimes = 0.723 oz silver. Washington Quarter (1932–1964) — 0.1808 troy oz pure silver. The most common junk silver coin. Four quarters ($1 face) = 0.7234 oz silver. Franklin Half Dollar (1948–1963) & Kennedy Half Dollar (1964) — 0.3617 troy oz pure silver. The 1964 Kennedy is 90% silver; all later Kennedys are not. Morgan Silver Dollar (1878–1921) & Peace Silver Dollar (1921–1935) — 0.7734 troy oz pure silver. The largest 90% coin by silver content. One dollar in face value gives you 0.7734 oz — slightly more than the standard $1.40 rule suggests for smaller coins, because dollars were less circulated. Barber Dimes, Quarters & Half Dollars (1892–1916) — Same silver content as their later equivalents. The Barber series is technically junk silver but often carries a slight collector premium due to age. 40% Silver Coins (1965–1970) Kennedy Half Dollar (1965–1970) — 0.1479 troy oz pure silver. Congress compromised with a 40% silver composition for halves when it eliminated silver from dimes and quarters. These are worth tracking separately since the $1.40 face-value rule does not apply to them. Eisenhower Dollar — Bicentennial Proof Sets — Some collector sets contain 40% silver Ikes. Circulated Eisenhower dollars are copper-nickel clad with no silver. 35% Silver Coins — War Nickels (1942–1945) Jefferson War Nickel (1942–1945) — 0.0563 troy oz pure silver. Identifiable by a large mint mark (P, D, or S) above Monticello on the reverse. The US added silver to nickels during WWII to conserve nickel for military use — one of history's more unusual monetary decisions. Despite their name, these are genuine silver coins worth accumulating. How to Calculate Junk Silver Value The melt value calculation is straightforward: Melt Value = Number of Coins × Silver Content (oz) × Spot Price ($/oz) For example, 20 pre-1965 quarters at a $34.50 spot price: 20 × 0.1808 × $34.50 = $124.75 melt value. The face value method is a useful shortcut for large mixed bags: multiply total face value by 0.715 (circulated) or 0.723 (uncirculated) to get troy ounces, then multiply by spot price. Junk Silver Face Value Calculator: What Dealers Actually Pay Knowing melt value is the starting point, but dealers don't pay spot. Here is what to realistically expect when selling junk silver: Local coin shops: Typically pay 85–95% of melt value, depending on volume and current demand. Small quantities fetch lower percentages. Online dealers (APMEX, JM Bullion, SD Bullion): Often pay 93–97% of melt for rolls and bags of $100+ face value. They post live buy prices on their websites. Pawn shops: Rarely pay more than 70–80% of melt. Only use as a last resort. eBay / peer-to-peer: Can achieve 100–110% of melt due to collector and investor demand, but fees, shipping, and time must be factored in. A practical rule: if a dealer offers you less than 90% of melt on a standard 90% bag, get a second quote. Competition among dealers is strong enough that fair pricing is readily available. Junk Silver vs. Silver Eagles: Which Is Better? Investors often debate junk silver versus American Silver Eagles (ASEs). Here is how they compare: Premium over spot: Junk silver typically carries a $1–3/oz premium. ASEs carry $4–8/oz or more. For pure silver exposure, junk silver is more cost-efficient. Liquidity: Both are highly liquid. ASEs are slightly easier to sell internationally due to universal recognition. Junk silver is universally recognized domestically. Divisibility: Junk silver wins. A dime = 0.0723 oz. An ASE = 1 full oz. In a barter scenario, small denomination junk silver is far more practical. Storage: Junk silver bags are bulkier per ounce than ASEs. A $1,000 face bag weighs about 55 lbs and contains ~715 oz of silver. Collectibility: Some junk silver pieces — particularly key dates like the 1916-D Mercury dime or 1921 Morgan — have significant numismatic value above melt. Always check for key dates before selling at melt. Key Dates to Check Before Selling Some coins that look like ordinary junk silver are worth dramatically more to collectors. Before selling any pre-1965 coin at melt value, check these key dates: Mercury Dimes: 1916-D, 1921, 1921-D (worth $500–$3,000+) Washington Quarters: 1932-D, 1932-S (worth $100–$500+) Morgan Dollars: 1893-S, 1895, 1889-CC (worth thousands) Standing Liberty Quarters: 1916, 1918/7-S (high premiums) A quick check on PCGS CoinFacts or NGC's price guide costs nothing and could save you from unknowingly selling a $500 coin for $6 in silver melt value. Frequently Asked Questions How much silver is in $1 face value of junk silver coins? Circulated 90% silver coins contain approximately 0.715 troy ounces of pure silver per $1.00 of face value. Uncirculated coins contain slightly more — 0.723 oz — because they haven't worn down. Our face value calculator uses 0.715 oz as the standard for mixed circulated bags, which is the accepted industry standard. What years of coins are junk silver? US dimes, quarters, and half dollars dated 1964 or earlier are 90% silver. Kennedy half dollars from 1965–1970 are 40% silver. Morgan and Peace silver dollars (all dates) are 90% silver. War Nickels dated 1942–1945 with a large mint mark are 35% silver. Coins dated 1971 or later (except special proof sets) contain no silver. Is junk silver a good investment in 2025–2026? Junk silver offers a low-premium entry point to silver ownership with excellent divisibility and universal recognition. It does not outperform spot silver — it tracks it closely. The primary appeal is owning government-issued silver in small denominations at minimal premium over raw metal value. For pure price appreciation, all forms of silver perform similarly; junk silver's edge is in cost efficiency and practicality. Should I clean my junk silver coins before selling? Never clean silver coins. Cleaning removes the natural patina (toning) and leaves hairline scratches visible under magnification. Even on "junk" coins with no collector value, cleaning is irreversible and can destroy any latent numismatic premium. Dealers and graders can immediately identify cleaned coins. Sell them as-found. How do I identify a silver coin without a scale? The simplest test: pre-1965 US coins have a solid silver edge with no copper stripe visible. Post-1965 clad coins show a visible orange-brown copper layer on the edge. A genuine silver coin also produces a distinctive high-pitched ring when dropped on a hard surface — the "ping test" — versus the dull thud of a clad coin. For confirmation, a neodymium magnet will not attract silver (silver is non-magnetic).
