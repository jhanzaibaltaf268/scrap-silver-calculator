Understanding Sterling Silver

Sterling silver is the most widely traded silver alloy in the world — an exact blend of 92.5% pure silver and 7.5% other metals, almost always copper. That copper addition is not an accident or a cost-cutting measure. Pure silver at .999 fineness has a Mohs hardness of just 2.5, softer than a human fingernail. Left unalloyed, silver bends under ordinary use, loses its shape, and scratches at the slightest contact. The 7.5% copper raises hardness significantly while preserving the material's workability and luster.

Modern metallurgy has introduced variants: Argentium sterling replaces some or all of the copper with germanium, and sometimes adds platinum or silicon, to dramatically reduce tarnishing. Argentium pieces typically carry an Argentium hallmark or "AG925" stamp alongside the standard 925 mark. For melt-value purposes, all 925 alloys — traditional or Argentium — return the same .925 calculation.

The name "sterling" derives from "Easterlings," German Baltic merchants whose high-quality silver coins became the benchmark for quality silver in medieval England. The 92.5% standard was formally codified by English law in 1300 AD under the Statute of Goldsmiths and has remained exactly there for over 700 years — one of the oldest continuously enforced quality standards in the world. It predates the United States, the metric system, and virtually every modern institution.

Hallmark identification is straightforward. American pieces must be stamped 925, .925, STERLING, STER, or STG. British sterling must be hallmarked at official assay offices — the four active ones are London (leopard's head), Birmingham (anchor), Edinburgh (castle), and Sheffield (York rose) — and every hallmarked piece carries a lion passant (walking lion) alongside the 925 mark. Sterling appears in virtually all silver jewelry from reputable retailers, nearly all silver flatware and hollowware produced in the US and UK since the 1860s, tea services, pitchers, candlesticks, and a vast range of antique decorative items.

What Makes Sterling Silver Special

The copper alloy makes sterling exceptional in one critical way: workability. Sterling can be cast, forged, drawn into wire, soldered, rolled flat, stamped with dies, and formed into complex three-dimensional shapes while holding its form through each stage. A silversmith working with .999 fine silver would find the material collapsing under tools; the same piece in sterling responds predictably and holds crisp detail.

Unlike gold alloys — where added copper actually reduces tarnishing — sterling silver tarnishes when copper is present. Silver sulfide forms on the surface when sterling is exposed to airborne sulfur compounds, which appear in rubber, wool, eggs, and certain paints. The result is a darkening layer that develops over weeks or months depending on storage and environment.

The critical point for anyone calculating scrap value: tarnish is a surface film only. It does not penetrate the silver, does not reduce silver content, and has zero effect on melt value. A heavily tarnished piece and a freshly polished piece of identical weight return identical values at the refiner. Many sellers wrongly assume their tarnished pieces are low-grade silver — they are not. Tarnish removes easily with silver polish or the aluminum foil electrochemical method, restoring full surface brightness.

Sterling Silver vs Pure Silver (.999)

Fine silver (.999) contains 99.9% pure silver per gram, while sterling (.925) contains 92.5%. The difference is 7.4% less silver per gram in sterling — which directly reduces melt value by that same proportion. At any given spot price, sterling's per-gram value equals fine silver's per-gram value multiplied by 0.925.

In practice, both standards have genuine markets. Sterling commands premium over spot as fabricated jewelry and flatware. Fine silver is the preferred bullion standard — investment-grade rounds, bars, and ETF holdings are all .999 or .9999. Large quantities of sterling scrap are commercially refined to .999 fine for return to the bullion market: refiners buy sterling at the .925 rate, recover pure silver, and sell it at spot, keeping the processing margin as their fee. This is why high-volume sellers achieve better net returns through refiners than through retail dealers, despite the additional handling step.

When comparing prices quoted by dealers, always confirm which purity standard is being applied. A quote referencing "fine silver per gram" does not equal a quote referencing "sterling silver per gram." The calculator on this page applies the .925 multiplier automatically.

Caring for Sterling Silver

The single most common misconception among people bringing in sterling silver is that tarnished pieces are worth less than polished ones. They are not — and understanding why removes any anxiety about the condition of your silver before a sale.

Tarnish is silver sulfide (Ag₂S), formed when sterling silver reacts with sulfur-containing gases in the air. The reaction is purely superficial — it creates a thin, dark film on the surface that does not penetrate the silver and does not reduce its weight or silver content in any meaningful way. A dealer or refiner weighing tarnished sterling silver arrives at the same calculation as one weighing a polished piece of identical mass.

If you want to remove tarnish before selling or for aesthetic reasons: the aluminum foil bath method is chemical-free and effective. Line a pan with aluminum foil (shiny side up), add boiling water, dissolve a tablespoon of baking soda per liter of water, then submerge the silver for 30–60 seconds. The electrochemical reaction converts silver sulfide back to metallic silver. Rinse and dry immediately. Commercial silver polish with a soft cloth also works for light tarnish.

Storage guidance: keep sterling in anti-tarnish cloth bags, sealed zip-lock bags with silica gel desiccant, or anti-tarnish chest drawer liners. Avoid rubber bands and rubber-lined drawers entirely — rubber off-gasses sulfur compounds rapidly. Keep sterling away from wool and eggs for the same reason. One important exception: never use abrasive cleaners or aggressive polishing on collector coins. Scratching reduces numismatic value even when melt value is unaffected.

How to Identify Sterling Silver — Three Methods

Before running a weight calculation, you need to confirm your item is actually sterling. Three methods work in practice, each with different cost, accuracy, and equipment requirements.

Method 1 — Hallmark inspection (free, non-destructive). A 10× jeweler's loupe or magnifying glass is all you need. The stamp location varies by item type: on rings and bracelets, check the inner band; on necklaces, check the clasp; on earrings, check the post or earwire; on flatware, check the back of the handle near where it meets the bowl or tine end; on hollowware like pitchers and tea sets, check the rim, base, or underside. Look for: 925, .925, STERLING, STER, or STG (American markings). British sterling will show a lion passant (a walking lion facing left) alongside the 925 number, plus a maker's mark and the assay office symbol: leopard's head for London, an anchor for Birmingham, a castle for Edinburgh, and a York rose for Sheffield. Absence of a hallmark does not mean the piece is not sterling — antique American items often predate the 1906 National Stamping Act requirements — but it does mean you should verify by other methods.

Method 2 — Magnet test (free, non-destructive). Sterling silver is not magnetic. Hold a rare earth magnet (neodymium) near the piece: silver, copper, gold, and most precious metals show zero attraction. Strong attraction indicates steel, iron, or nickel-based metals — none of which are sterling. The limitation of this test: it cannot distinguish sterling from silver-plated base metals (EPNS — electroplated nickel silver) or from other non-magnetic base metals. A piece that passes the magnet test may still be silver-plated rather than solid sterling. Use this as an elimination test, not a confirmation.

Method 3 — Acid test (definitive DIY identification). Silver testing kits cost $8–15 and use nitric acid solutions calibrated for different silver purities. Apply a small drop to a hidden area of the piece (or a thin scraping on a testing stone). .925 sterling produces a cream or off-white color reaction with the standard silver acid. Lower purities (800, 835) produce progressively darker gray reactions. Base metals produce green or blue-green. This test is 98%+ accurate and is the definitive at-home identification method. For high-value or unmarked pieces, professional XRF (X-ray fluorescence) testing at a coin dealer or gold buyer delivers the exact alloy composition in under 30 seconds with zero damage to the piece.

What Dealers Pay for Sterling Silver Scrap

The fair offer range for sterling silver scrap is 80–92% of melt, and the variation within that range is driven by three factors: buyer type, item characteristics, and quantity. Understanding each factor lets you position your lot to attract the best offers.

Item characteristics that reduce payout: mixed lots containing items with uncertain purity, pieces with significant solder fills (common in antique repair work — solder adds non-silver weight), damaged clasps or settings with embedded stones that must be removed before refining, and small individual pieces under 10 grams where handling cost is disproportionate to silver content.

Item characteristics that increase payout: all pieces clearly stamped 925 or STERLING, lots containing a single item type (flatware only, or jewelry only), items over 100 grams that require minimal sorting, and clean flatware from identifiable patterns where the dealer can assess purity instantly.

Negotiation is both possible and expected in this market. Most dealers' first offer is below their maximum. Know your melt value from this calculator before walking in — or before responding to any offer — and present it as your reference point. An offer of 80% of melt is reasonable but often negotiable upward to 85–88% with a clean, large lot. Always get at least two quotes from different buyer types before committing.
