Understanding Silver Purity

Silver purity, also called "fineness," describes the proportion of pure silver in an alloy. It's expressed as parts per thousand — for example, 925 means 925 parts silver out of 1,000.

Common Silver Purities Explained

999 Fine Silver (99.9%)

The purest commercial form of silver. Used in bullion bars, investment-grade coins (American Silver Eagles, Canadian Maple Leafs), and industrial applications. Too soft for jewelry.

958 Britannia Silver (95.8%)

A high-purity silver standard established in England in 1697. Still used today for fine British silverware and specialty items. Higher purity than sterling, but less common.

925 Sterling Silver (92.5%)

The world's most popular silver alloy. The standard for jewelry, flatware, and decorative items. Alloyed with copper for durability. More details: What Is Sterling Silver?

900 Coin Silver (90%)

The standard for US silver coinage before 1965 (dimes, quarters, halves, dollars). Also used in some historical jewelry marked "COIN". Calculate values: Junk Silver Calculator

835 Silver (83.5%)

A standard commonly found in Germany, the Netherlands, and other European countries. Used for silverware, small items, and some coinage.

800 Silver (80%)

Common European standard, especially in Italy, Germany, and Austria. Frequently found in antique silverware, serving pieces, and decorative items.

How to Read Silver Hallmarks

Silver items are typically stamped with a fineness number (925, 800, etc.) along with:

  • Maker's mark — Identifies the manufacturer
  • Assay office mark — Where the item was tested
  • Date letter — Year of manufacture (British system)
  • Country mark — Country of origin

Why Silver Purity Varies: Regional Standards Explained

Different regions adopted different silver standards based on their historical trade practices, monetary systems, and silversmithing traditions:

  • 925 (Sterling): Originated in medieval England and adopted globally. The standard in the UK, US, Canada, Australia, and most of Asia for jewelry and flatware.
  • 800: The dominant standard in Italy, Germany, Austria, and much of Continental Europe. Widely used in Victorian and Edwardian serving pieces. Contains 80% silver — still highly valuable but lower than sterling.
  • 835: Common in the Netherlands, parts of Germany, and Scandinavia. Sits between 800 and 925 in purity and value.
  • 900: The standard for pre-1965 US coins. Also used in some French and Latin American silverware. Easy to identify on coins by the date.
  • 958 (Britannia): Briefly mandatory in England from 1697–1720 (Parliament required it to stop silversmiths melting coins). Still used for specialty British silverware and commemorative items.
  • 999: Pure investment silver. Too soft for practical fabricated items — used exclusively for bullion bars, coins, and specialty applications.

Silver Purity Testing Methods

If a hallmark is missing, worn, or unclear, several testing methods can determine purity:

  • Acid test (nitric acid): Apply a drop of nitric acid to a small scratch on the surface. The color of the reaction indicates purity — cream/white = high silver; green = base metal; no reaction = fine silver. Test kits cost $10–$20 at jeweler supply stores.
  • XRF (X-Ray Fluorescence) analysis: The most accurate non-destructive method. A gun-shaped device shoots X-rays at the surface and reads the precise element composition. Available at most refineries free of charge for sellers, or at pawnbrokers and coin shops.
  • Specific gravity test: Weigh the item dry, then weigh it suspended in water. Pure silver has a specific gravity of 10.49; the ratio of dry to underwater weight reveals the purity. Requires a precision scale and patience.
  • Magnet test: Silver is not magnetic. If a magnet strongly attracts the item, it is not silver. However, passing the magnet test does not confirm silver — other non-magnetic metals also pass. Use as a quick elimination test only.
  • Ice test: Silver has the highest thermal conductivity of any metal. Place an ice cube on a flat silver surface — it melts visibly faster than on other metals. Useful for identifying larger flat items.

For valuable or large quantities, XRF testing at a reputable refiner is the gold standard — it provides a certified purity reading that serves as documentation for the sale.

Purity and Dealer Offers: What to Expect

When selling silver by purity, dealers typically quote a percentage of the published spot price. The actual offer depends on buyer type, quantity, and condition, but as a general guide:

  • Online refineries: 90–98% of melt value. Best for bulk lots. Processing takes 5–10 days.
  • Coin and silver dealers: 75–90% of melt value. Good for quick same-day sales with negotiation.
  • Local jewelers: 65–80% of melt value. Convenient but rarely the highest offer.
  • Pawn shops: 50–70% of melt value. Last resort — only for immediate cash emergencies.

Always calculate your melt value first using the calculators linked above, then compare dealer offers as a percentage of that baseline. See our complete guide to selling silver for step-by-step advice.

Silver Fineness vs. Karat: What's the Difference?

Silver purity is expressed in fineness (parts per 1,000), while gold purity uses the karat system (parts per 24). There is no "karat" system for silver — if you see a silver item stamped with a karat number, it is either a forgery or a combination piece (e.g., gold and silver mixed).

The key conversion: fineness ÷ 10 = percentage purity. So 925 = 92.5%, 800 = 80.0%, 999 = 99.9%.
