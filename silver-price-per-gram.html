Understanding this purity table is the essential first step for anyone valuing silver jewelry, scrap silver, or bullion. The purity decimal system is straightforward: a purity of .925 means 92.5 parts pure silver per 100 parts of alloy. To calculate the per gram price for any purity, you multiply the fine silver per-gram price by that decimal. The remaining metal content — typically copper in sterling silver — adds structural strength but has negligible value compared to the silver content.

The table displays four unit columns because different silver markets use different conventions. The gram is the practical retail and scrap standard — most jewelry scales measure in grams to 0.01g precision. The troy ounce (31.1035 grams) is the global commodity market standard; every COMEX and LBMA quote is in troy ounces. The kilogram is the wholesale and industrial standard used by refiners, ETF custodians, and electronics manufacturers. Pennyweight (DWT) is the traditional jewelers' unit: 20 DWT equals one troy ounce, and many pawnbrokers and older jewelry appraisers still quote in DWT.

All four columns show the same underlying silver value expressed in different measurement scales — a 31.1035-gram piece of .925 sterling silver is worth exactly the same whether you quote it as a per-gram rate, a per-ounce rate, or a per-kilogram rate. The only difference is the number used. Knowing which unit a potential dealer is quoting prevents significant valuation errors, particularly on larger bar or flatware transactions.

One critical warning: items marked EPNS (Electro Plated Nickel Silver), Silver Plate, Sheffield Plate, or German Silver are not included in this table because they contain essentially no silver. The silver layer on electroplated pieces is measured in microns, not grams, and has no meaningful scrap value. Check your hallmark carefully before using any row in this table — look for .925, 925, STERLING, STER, .999, or the specific purity number stamped on the piece.

Silver Price Per Gram vs Troy Ounce vs Kilogram vs DWT

Four weight units dominate the global silver market, and knowing which one is being used in any given quote is essential for accurate silver valuation. Confusing them can produce errors ranging from 9.7% (mixing up troy ounce and standard ounce) to a factor of 32 (mixing up gram and kilogram). On a $1,000 silver transaction, a 9.7% error means $97 miscalculated — a significant mistake that favors whoever is quoting in the unfamiliar unit.

The troy ounce (31.1035 grams) is the global commodity market standard. Every COMEX futures contract, every LBMA quote, and every bullion dealer price list uses the troy ounce as the baseline unit. The standard avoirdupois ounce used for groceries and household goods (28.3495 grams) is never used in silver pricing — using the wrong ounce produces a 9.7% error. If a buyer quotes you a silver price per "ounce" without specifying troy, it is almost certainly a troy ounce price — but it is worth confirming, particularly on large quantities of scrap silver or bar silver.

The gram is the practical retail and scrap unit. Most jewelry scales are calibrated in grams; most scrap silver buyers and refiners quote scrap prices per gram. If you are selling jewelry, flatware, or other small silver items, you will almost always be quoted a per-gram rate. Pennyweight (DWT) is the traditional jewelers' unit — 20 DWT equals one troy ounce, so 1 DWT = 1.55517 grams. Many older pawnbrokers and some estate jewelry buyers still quote in pennyweight. A buyer offering "$1.80 per DWT" for sterling is offering 1.80 × 20 = $36.00 per troy ounce for .999 silver equivalent — compare to the spot price of $— per troy ounce to evaluate the offer.

How to Find Your Silver's Purity Before Looking Up Its Per-Gram Value

Before using the purity table above, you need to identify which row applies to your silver piece. This sounds simple, but missing or worn hallmarks and the prevalence of silver-plated items mean that identification is genuinely the first and most important step in any scrap silver or jewelry valuation. Getting the purity wrong — for example, treating .800 European silverware as .925 sterling — produces a 15.6% overvaluation error. Using this table for silver-plated items produces a 100% error, since plated pieces contain negligible silver content.

The five most common identification scenarios are: (1) Jewelry stamped 925, STERLING, or STER → use the .925 sterling silver row. (2) Bullion bar or round stamped 999 or Fine Silver → use the .999 row. (3) US coins dated 1964 or earlier (dimes, quarters, halves, dollars) → use the .900 coin silver row — no additional stamp needed, as the US Mint guaranteed the composition. (4) European silverware or jewelry stamped 800 or 835 → use the .800 or .835 row respectively. (5) Items marked EPNS, A1, Silver Plate, 1/20, WM, or Sheffield Plate → not in the table; these are plated and contain essentially no pure silver.

For practical identification, use a 10× loupe to read the small stamps on clasps, the backs of flatware handles, and ring shanks — hallmarks can be very small. A neodymium magnet is a quick first test: genuine silver is non-magnetic, while stainless steel and some base metals are strongly magnetic. For unmarked pieces, a silver acid test kit ($8–15 from any jewelry supply store) confirms silver content in about 30 seconds by testing a small surface scratch.
