The melt value formula for any silver bar has three steps. Most errors occur in Step 1 — the unit conversion — because silver spot price is always quoted per troy ounce, a unit that most people do not use in everyday life. Understanding all three steps lets you verify any dealer's offer and confirm you are being quoted correctly. These steps apply equally to silver bars, coins, bullion rounds, and raw silver grain.

Silver Bar Premiums vs Melt Value

Every new silver bar sells at a premium above melt value. That premium covers the refiner's fabrication cost, the distributor's margin, the dealer's retail markup, and in some cases the brand value of the refiner. Understanding premiums is essential for evaluating whether you are paying a fair price and for estimating realistic sell-back proceeds. The melt value from this calculator is always the theoretical floor — the actual transaction price on both buying and selling sides differs from melt in predictable ways.

On the buying side: a 1 oz silver bar typically carries a 4–10% premium over melt value. A 100 oz bar drops to 0.5–1.5%. A 400 oz Good Delivery bar trades at or very near spot price with almost zero percentage premium. The PAMP Suisse Fortuna series carries a modest additional premium due to its iconic Lakshmi design and the included assay card with serial number — which also makes it one of the most instantly verifiable bullion products worldwide.

On the selling side: dealers pay below melt value to preserve their own resale margin. For recognized .999 fine silver bars from PAMP Suisse, Valcambi, Engelhard, Johnson Matthey, Perth Mint, and Sunshine Minting, expect 97–99% of melt value at major online bullion buyers. Unbranded or unknown poured bars may receive only 90–95% until independently verified with XRF testing. The key takeaway: brand provenance matters at resale. The melt value this calculator shows sets your ceiling for what you will receive — and your floor for what you should pay when buying. Identifying Silver Bar Purity and Authenticity

Every legitimate investment-grade silver bar must display four pieces of information: fineness (stamped as .999 FINE SILVER, 999/1000, or .9999), weight in troy ounces or grams, the refiner's name and logo, and a unique serial number on any bar weighing 10 troy oz or more. The absence of a serial number on a 10+ oz bar should prompt verification. Major refiners also incorporate anti-counterfeiting features: PAMP Suisse uses kinegrams (holographic security features embedded in the bar's surface), Valcambi uses micro-engraved text readable only under magnification, and several refiners now include QR codes linking to assay records.

Spotting counterfeit silver bars requires checking several things simultaneously. Weight must match the stamped value — a genuine 10 oz bar weighs 311.035 grams exactly. Any significant deviation indicates a problem. Silver is completely non-magnetic — a bar attracted to a strong neodymium magnet contains ferrous metal and is counterfeit or heavily debased. Logos must have the correct proportions and depth; shallow, soft-edged stamping on a bar claiming to be from PAMP or Engelhard is a warning sign.

The most reliable non-destructive test is density measurement using Archimedes' principle: weigh the bar in air, then weigh it submerged in water. Pure silver has a density of 10.49 g/cm³. The most common counterfeit material — tungsten — has a density of 19.3 g/cm³ (almost twice silver's density), making it easily detectable by weight discrepancy in a full-sized bar. XRF (X-ray fluorescence) testing at reputable bullion dealers is typically free or costs $5 and reads purity to four decimal places in under 60 seconds. For large transactions, always insist on XRF testing before finalizing price.

Silver Bars vs Silver Coins vs Silver Rounds

Silver bars, government-minted coins, and private-mint rounds all contain .999 fine silver in their standard investment forms, but they differ significantly in premiums, liquidity, and resale dynamics. Silver bars offer the lowest premiums for equivalent silver weight — a 10 oz bar typically carries $8–15 in total premium above melt, compared to 10 individual 1 oz rounds at $2–4 each ($20–40 total) or 10 individual American Silver Eagle coins at $5–10 each ($50–100 total). For investors whose primary goal is maximum pure silver per dollar spent, bars consistently win on purchase price.

Government coins — American Silver Eagles, Canadian Maple Leafs, Australian Kangaroos — carry the highest premiums but offer maximum liquidity and universal recognition. Any coin dealer, pawn shop, or online bullion buyer recognizes these instantly without testing. Private-mint rounds sit between bars and government coins: premiums are lower than Eagles but slightly higher than bars, and liquidity is nearly equivalent to bars at reputable bullion dealers. For scrapping all three forms — whether a silver bar, round, or coin — recognized .999 fine silver from legitimate sources returns 95–99% of melt value at major online bullion buyers.
