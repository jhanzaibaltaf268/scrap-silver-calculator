What Affects Silver Prices?

1. Industrial Demand

Silver is used in electronics (largest industrial use), solar panels, medical devices, and photography. Demand from these industries directly impacts price.

2. Investment Demand

Investors buy silver as a safe-haven asset during economic uncertainty, inflation concerns, or market volatility. This creates upward price pressure.

3. Supply Constraints

Silver is a byproduct of copper and zinc mining. Limited mining output or supply chain disruptions can reduce availability and raise prices.

4. US Dollar Strength

Silver is priced in USD. A stronger dollar makes silver more expensive for foreign buyers, potentially reducing demand and lowering prices.

5. Economic Growth

Strong economic growth increases industrial demand. Economic slowdowns can reduce both industrial and investment demand.

6. Geopolitical Events

War, trade tensions, or political instability often increase demand for precious metals as safe-haven assets.

7. Inflation Expectations

Rising inflation concerns make precious metals attractive stores of value, increasing demand and price.

💡 Pro Tip: Silver prices are most volatile during economic uncertainty. Check prices regularly when selling scrap silver—prices can swing $2–5 per ounce in a single week.

Where to Check Silver Prices

Official Market Data Sources

  • COMEX (Commodity Exchange): US futures exchange with official daily silver prices
  • London Bullion Market (LBMA): Global benchmark for precious metals spot prices
  • Trading Economics: Real-time spot prices aggregated from global markets
  • Kitco: Live price charts and market analysis
  • APMEX & JM Bullion: Dealer spot prices (typically include markup)

Important Notes

  • Spot price: The wholesale market price for bulk silver (what we show above)
  • Dealer price: Retail prices from bullion dealers (includes markup, typically 15–25% above spot)
  • Scrap price: What dealers pay for scrap silver (typically 70–95% of spot price)

Frequently Asked Questions About Silver Prices

How do I calculate silver value from spot price?

Multiply the item's weight in troy ounces by its purity (as a decimal), then multiply by the spot price. Formula: Value = Weight × Purity × Spot Price. For example: 10 oz of .925 silver × .925 × $83.68 = $772.21

Why is my local dealer's price different from the spot price?

Dealers add markup when selling (typically 15–25% above spot) and pay less when buying scrap (typically 70–95% of spot). This margin covers their refining, shipping, insurance, and profit.

What time of day is silver price lowest?

Silver prices fluctuate 24/7 as trading moves between time zones. Typically, prices are lowest during Asian trading hours (evening US time) and highest during US/UK trading hours (morning-afternoon US time). However, price swings are unpredictable and driven by news, supply, and demand.

Should I sell my silver today or wait?

This depends on your financial goals and market outlook. Silver prices are volatile—they can swing $2–5 per ounce in days. Dealers buy and sell daily, so you won't "miss" the price. Focus on getting quotes from multiple buyers rather than timing the market.

Do silver prices move with gold prices?

Silver and gold are both precious metals and respond to similar macroeconomic forces (inflation, economic growth, geopolitical risk). However, silver also has significant industrial demand, so it can move independently based on manufacturing outlook.

Is the spot price the same worldwide?

Yes, the spot price is set globally by commodity markets and is the same worldwide at any given moment. However, local dealers add their own markups based on shipping costs, currency, demand, and competition.

How accurate is the price shown on your site?

Our price is updated every hour from Trading Economics, which aggregates data from global commodity markets (COMEX, London Bullion Market, OTC). It is accurate within 15–60 minutes. For real-time intra-day prices, check COMEX futures or dealer sites directly.

What is "junk silver"?

Junk silver refers to older US coins (pre-1964 dimes, quarters, half-dollars, and dollars) that contain 90% silver. They are called "junk" because they are worth more for their silver content than their face value, not because they are low quality.
