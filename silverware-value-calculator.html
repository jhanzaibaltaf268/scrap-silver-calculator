How to Value Silverware

The value of silverware depends critically on one factor above all others: is it solid silver or silver-plated? Solid silver items carry significant melt value tied to the live silver spot price. Silver-plated items — which have only a microscopic coating of silver over a base metal — have almost no scrap value regardless of age or appearance.

This calculator uses the formula: Melt Value = (Weight ÷ 31.1035) × Purity × Spot Price. Select the silverware type for an average pre-filled weight, or enter the exact weight from your scale for a precise result.

Famous Silverware Makers and Pattern Value

Solid silver flatware and hollowware from prestigious makers can be worth significantly more than their melt value, particularly in complete sets or rare patterns. Before scrapping, identify the maker's mark — usually a separate small stamp near the purity hallmark:

  • Tiffany & Co. — American sterling pieces are highly collectible. A complete 12-person set can fetch $3,000–$15,000+ depending on the pattern.
  • Georg Jensen — Danish sterling; iconic patterns like Acorn and Blossom command 5–15× melt value at auction.
  • Gorham Manufacturing — One of America's most prolific sterling producers. Common patterns (Chantilly, Strasbourg) sell for 2–4× melt; rare patterns more.
  • Reed & Barton — Quality American sterling; Francis I pattern is especially sought by collectors.
  • Wallace, Towle, Whiting — American makers with established collector markets. Pattern identification is key to value.
  • English makers (Mappin & Webb, Elkington, Sheffield makers) — British sterling with date hallmarks often carries antique premiums.

If you find a maker's mark, search the pattern name on eBay "Sold Listings" before scrapping. You may find your 40g sterling spoon is worth $45 in melt — but $180 in collector value.

Selling Silverware: Best Practices

  • Weigh everything first — A digital scale accurate to 1 gram works well for silverware. Group by purity grade.
  • Separate solid from plated — Never mix EPNS with sterling; it dramatically reduces your offer.
  • Keep sets together — A complete 12-person set with all pieces sells for significantly more than individual pieces.
  • Research pattern names — Check eBay sold listings and specialist sites like Replacements Ltd. to assess collector value.
  • Get multiple quotes — Online refineries pay the most for straight scrap; antique dealers may pay more for complete sets in named patterns.

Beware Weighted Handles and Filled Bases

This is the single biggest mistake people make when valuing inherited silverware. Many sterling pieces are not solid silver throughout. To give them balance and stability, makers fill them with cement, pitch, or rosin, and you must subtract that filler before calculating melt value:

  • Knife handles - sterling knives have hollow handles filled with cement, plus a stainless steel blade. Only the thin silver shell counts. A "sterling" dinner knife weighing 80-120 g may hold only 25-40 g of actual silver.
  • Candlestick bases and weighted compotes - often stamped "weighted" or "reinforced." The base is packed with filler, so weighted candlesticks may be only 10-25% silver by total weight. Never price these by total weight.
No mark? Assume plate until proven otherwise

Genuine sterling is almost always stamped. Unmarked flatware is usually plate. A magnet test helps - silver is not magnetic, so if a magnet grabs it, the core is base metal. For a definitive answer, a coin shop can run a quick XRF scan.

Complete Set vs Incomplete Set: Value Impact

A complete, matched set for 8 or 12 in a desirable pattern, ideally with its original chest, sells to collectors and replacement buyers well above melt. An incomplete or mismatched lot - odd spoons, a few forks, no knives - usually sells at or just below melt because the buyer will likely scrap it. Count your place settings and serving pieces before deciding whether to sell as a set or by weight. A classic place setting (dinner fork, salad fork, dinner knife, teaspoon, soup spoon) yields roughly 150-200 g of actual silver once knife filler is excluded.

What Silver Buyers Pay for Flatware

For melt-grade sterling flatware (common patterns, incomplete sets), expect buyers to pay 80-90% of melt value, since they must account for knife filler, refining, and resale. Refiners handling bulk lots may pay closer to 90-95%. Collectible complete sets are priced by pattern and condition, not by melt, and can fetch several times the metal value. Always calculate the melt value with the tool above first so you know your floor before accepting any offer.

Silverware Value FAQ

How much is sterling silver flatware worth?

It is worth its melt value - weight x 0.925 x spot price - minus the buyer's margin. A typical place setting holds 150-200 g of recoverable silver. Buyers usually pay 80-90% of melt for common patterns; collectible complete sets can be worth much more.

How do I know if my silverware is real silver or plated?

Check the marks. "STERLING" or "925" means solid silver; "EPNS," "EP," "A1," or "SILVER PLATE" means plated with little melt value. Unmarked pieces are usually plate. A magnet test and an XRF scan confirm it.

Why don't sterling knives count their full weight?

Sterling knife handles are hollow and filled with cement for balance, and the blade is stainless steel. Only the thin silver shell is recoverable, so an 80-120 g knife may hold only 25-40 g of actual silver.

Should I sell my silver flatware as a set or for scrap?

If it is a complete, matched set in a desirable pattern (Chantilly, Francis I, Repousse), selling intact usually beats melt. Incomplete or common-pattern lots typically sell at melt, so scrapping is fine.

What is 800 silver on flatware?

800 (and 835) silver is European continental silver that is 80% (or 83.5%) pure. It is genuine solid silver, just lower purity than 925 sterling, so its melt value is proportionally lower.

Is silver-plated flatware worth anything?

Very little for its silver content - the plating is only microns thick. Silver-plate may still have resale value as usable or antique flatware, but it has essentially no melt value compared with solid sterling.
