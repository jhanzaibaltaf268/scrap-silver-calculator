What Is 925 Sterling Silver? — 92.5% Pure, Durable, and Globally Recognized

925 sterling silver — stamped "925", "Sterling", or ".925" — is an alloy containing 92.5% pure silver and 7.5% other metals, almost always copper. It is the international standard for jewelry, flatware, and decorative silver, used by designers from Tiffany & Co. to Georg Jensen and every silversmith in between. The name "sterling" traces back to medieval Europe, where English silversmiths developed the standard for coinage and trade; today it is legally mandated in the US, UK, EU, and most major markets as the minimum purity threshold for items sold as "silver."

The copper addition is not a flaw — it's intentional. Pure 999 fine silver is soft enough to bend with your fingers, which makes it impractical for rings, bracelets, and forks that endure daily stress. By adding 7.5% copper, sterling silver achieves the hardness needed for wearable, functional items while keeping silver content high enough to matter for scrap value and collectibility. The trade-off is susceptibility to tarnish, since copper oxidizes faster than silver alone.

Why 925 Sterling Silver Has Real Scrap and Resale Value

Most people discovering a box of old jewelry or a set of inherited flatware don't realize what it could be worth. At typical silver prices, a single heavy sterling silver bracelet can contain $15–$40 worth of silver. A complete 12-piece flatware service can hold $300–$800 in melt value alone. Here's why that matters.

High Silver Content by Weight

92.5% silver content is substantial. For every 10 grams of sterling silver you own, 9.25 grams is pure silver. At current spot prices, that translates to meaningful cash — especially for heavier items like candlesticks, tea sets, and dinner plates. Most people are surprised at how much their silverware is worth when they run the numbers.

  • →9.25g pure silver per 10g of sterling
  • →Heavier items yield more melt value
  • →Flatware often outweighs jewelry by 3–10×

Globally Recognized Standard

The 925 sterling standard is understood by jewelers, coin dealers, pawn shops, and online buyers worldwide. Unlike unmarked or unusual silver alloys that require acid testing, a clear 925 stamp means buyers don't need to verify purity before making an offer — which speeds up the sale and often improves the price you receive.

  • →Accepted at any coin dealer or jeweler
  • →Legal standard in US, UK, EU, and beyond
  • →No acid testing required with clear hallmark

Resale Beyond Scrap

Not all 925 silver should be scrapped. Brand-name and antique sterling pieces — Tiffany, Georg Jensen, Gorham, Wallace, Reed & Barton — often sell for 2–10× their melt value to collectors. A Tiffany sterling bracelet worth $30 at melt might fetch $150–$300 on eBay or at auction. Always check resale value before selling to a scrap buyer.

  • →Named brands command collector premiums
  • →Antique pieces may have numismatic value
  • →Resale platforms often beat scrap by 100–500%

How to Identify 925 Sterling Silver — Hallmarks, Tests & Fakes

Knowing whether your silver is genuinely 925 is the first step before calculating value or selling. Authentic sterling silver carries specific marks, and there are simple at-home tests you can use to distinguish real sterling from silver-plated or silver-colored base metals.

Official Hallmarks to Look For

Authentic 925 silver is always marked by the manufacturer before leaving the workshop. The hallmark is typically stamped with a tool called a punch and appears in a small, precise area — not printed or painted. Look carefully with a loupe or magnifying glass.

  • →Rings & bands: inside the shank (inner band)
  • →Necklaces & pendants: on the clasp or tag near clasp
  • →Bracelets: on the clasp or inside the link
  • →Flatware: underside of handle, near the neck
  • →Dishes & trays: flat underside, near edge or center

Accepted Stamps That Mean Sterling

Several different stamps all indicate genuine 925 sterling silver. Each is legally recognized in its respective market, so seeing any of these on your item confirms its purity. Older pieces may use text instead of numbers — particularly British and American silver from before 1970.

  • ✓"925" — most common modern mark worldwide
  • ✓"Sterling" — traditional US and UK designation
  • ✓"Ster" or ".925" — abbreviated US marks
  • ✓Lion passant (UK) — British assay office hallmark
  • ✓S925, Ag925 — Asian and international variants

At-Home Tests for Sterling Silver

Before spending money on professional assaying, these simple tests help you quickly screen items. None of these are 100% conclusive alone, but combined with visual hallmark inspection they give you strong confidence about whether what you have is real sterling silver.

  • →Magnet test: real silver is not magnetic. If it sticks, it's likely plated base metal.
  • →Ice test: silver conducts heat exceptionally — ice melts visibly fast on genuine sterling.
  • →Polish test: rub with a white cloth. Real tarnish comes off as black marks on the cloth.
  • →Acid test: a 925 acid test kit ($5–$15) gives definitive results — green reaction = genuine silver.

Where to Sell 925 Sterling Silver — 3 Options Compared

Getting the best price for your sterling silver depends on where you sell. The difference between the highest and lowest buyer can be 40–50% of melt value — potentially hundreds of dollars on a large lot. Here's what to expect from each channel.

Best Price

Online Silver Buyers

Specialized online precious metals buyers like Abe Mor, Worthy, or online coin dealers compete aggressively for sterling silver scrap. Because they refine at scale, they can afford to pay more than walk-in buyers. Mail-in services typically pay 80–88% of melt value for sterling jewelry and flatware, with free shipping kits and fast payment.

  • ✓Highest payout for scrap sellers
  • ✓Free insured shipping (most programs)
  • ✓Check or wire payment in 3–5 business days
Fast Payment

Local Coin & Jewelry Dealers

Local coin shops and jewelers offer the fastest transactions — typically walk in, get weighed, receive cash or check within 30 minutes. Payouts range from 65–82% of melt value depending on the dealer and your relationship with them. Bringing multiple quotes from competing dealers often gets you a better offer, as local buyers can negotiate.

  • ✓Same-day cash payment
  • ✓Dealer can identify items and advise on value
  • ✓Good for brand-name or designer pieces
Lowest Offer

Pawn Shops

Pawn shops are a last resort for sterling silver. They typically offer 40–65% of melt value because they need a large margin to stay profitable on quick-turnover loans and retail sales. Unless you need cash immediately and have no other option, the additional 15–30% you leave on the table by using a pawn shop instead of a coin dealer or online buyer adds up quickly on larger lots.

  • →Immediate cash if needed
  • →Option to reclaim items with loan (if pawning)
  • ✗Lowest payout — often 40–55% of melt

Frequently Asked Questions — 925 Sterling Silver

Answers to the most common questions from people selling, buying, or inheriting 925 sterling silver.

What is the price of 925 sterling silver per gram today? +

The 925 sterling silver price per gram is calculated as: (spot price per troy ounce ÷ 31.1035) × 0.925. At a spot price of $28/oz, that works out to approximately $0.833 per gram. The live price at the top of this page updates every hour from COMEX data.

What does 925 mean on silver jewelry? +

The 925 stamp on silver jewelry means the piece is made of 92.5% pure silver alloyed with 7.5% other metals (usually copper). This ratio is the international standard for "sterling silver." The number 925 represents 925 parts silver per 1,000 parts of metal. It is the most common silver standard for jewelry worldwide.

Is 925 silver worth anything as scrap? +

Yes — 925 sterling silver has real and meaningful scrap value. At a spot price of $28/oz, a 10-gram sterling ring contains about $7.50 worth of silver at melt. A 500-gram set of flatware could contain $375 worth of silver. Dealers typically pay 70–88% of melt value, depending on how you sell.

How do I calculate the melt value of 925 sterling silver? +

To manually calculate: multiply weight (grams) × 0.925 × (spot price ÷ 31.1035). Example: 20g × 0.925 × ($28 ÷ 31.1035) = 20 × 0.925 × $0.900 = $16.65. Our calculator above handles this automatically with live pricing — just enter the weight and unit.

Where can I sell 925 sterling silver for the best price? +

Online precious metals buyers offer the best scrap rates for 925 sterling silver — typically 80–88% of melt value. Local coin dealers pay 65–82% but give same-day cash. Pawn shops offer the least at 40–65%. If your pieces have brand names or collectible appeal, reselling on eBay or Etsy can significantly exceed scrap prices.

Why is 925 silver cheaper than 999 fine silver? +

925 sterling silver contains 7.5% non-silver alloy (mainly copper), which reduces its silver content per gram by 7.5% compared to pure 999 silver. Additionally, refineries charge to extract the pure silver from the copper alloy — this extraction cost gets subtracted from what dealers offer for scrap. Pure 999 bullion avoids this entirely, which is why it trades closer to spot.

How do I know if my silver is genuinely 925? +

Look for stamps: "925", "Sterling", "Ster", ".925", or the British lion passant assay mark. The stamp is usually inside a ring band, on a clasp, or on the underside of flatware. A magnet test confirms silver authenticity: real silver is non-magnetic. An acid test kit ($5–$15) provides definitive confirmation if you're unsure.

Does 925 sterling silver tarnish? +

Yes — 925 sterling tarnishes because its 7.5% copper content reacts with sulfur compounds in the air and skin to form silver sulfide, the dark layer you see. Tarnish is harmless and removable with silver polish or a baking soda paste. Store jewelry in anti-tarnish bags or add silica gel sachets to your jewelry box to slow tarnishing significantly.
