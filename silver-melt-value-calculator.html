What Does "Melt Value" Actually Mean?

Melt value is the worth of the pure silver metal inside an object if it were melted down and refined into bullion. It ignores the shape, brand, age, or craftsmanship of the item and counts only the raw silver. The math is the same for a sterling spoon, a broken chain, or a coin: you take the item's weight, multiply by its purity (the fraction that is actually silver), and multiply by the current spot price of silver. That gives the intrinsic metal value - the number this calculator returns the moment you enter weight and purity.

Understanding melt value is the single most useful skill when selling silver, because it is the one figure a buyer cannot argue with. The shape of a candlestick is subjective; the 142 grams of sterling inside it is not.

Melt Value vs Market, Retail, and Numismatic Value

People use "value" to mean four different things, and confusing them costs sellers money:

  • Melt value - the raw silver content at spot price. The floor for any genuine silver item.
  • Market (spot) value - what one troy ounce of pure silver trades for right now on global exchanges. Melt value is derived from it.
  • Retail value - what you would pay in a store for the finished item. Always far above melt because it includes manufacturing, branding, and markup. You will never get retail back when selling scrap.
  • Numismatic (collector) value - what a collector pays for rarity, condition, or history. This can be many times melt for a key-date coin or a sought-after antique, or zero extra for a common piece.

Why Melt Value Is the Floor Price

No honest bulk buyer pays less than melt minus a fair refining margin, because the silver itself can always be recovered and resold at spot. That makes melt value your negotiating anchor. A common piece of generic sterling is worth its melt value and little more; a buyer who offers far below melt is either lowballing you or doubts the metal is genuine. As a rule, expect refiners to pay 88-97% of melt on bulk lots and walk-in shops to pay 70-90%. Anything under about 70% of melt for confirmed silver is a poor deal.

Always calculate melt first

Before you accept any offer, run the weight and purity through the calculator above. If a dealer offers $90 for a lot with a $130 melt value, you instantly know that is only 69% of melt - and you can ask for more or walk away.

How Melt Value Is Calculated

The formula is:

Melt value = weight in troy ounces x purity x spot price

If your scale reads grams, divide grams by 31.1035 to get troy ounces first, or just enter grams into the calculator and pick a purity. Worked example at a $32 spot price: a 100 g sterling (92.5%) lot = (100 / 31.1035) x 0.925 x $32 = $95.20. The same 100 g in fine .999 silver = $102.80, and in 800 European silver = $82.32. Purity is what moves the number.

How the Spot Price Is Set

The "spot price" is the live global price for immediate delivery of one troy ounce of pure silver. It is driven mainly by two markets: the LBMA Silver Price, a benchmark auction run in London once each business day, and COMEX silver futures, which trade nearly around the clock in New York and set the minute-to-minute price most websites quote. Spot moves with supply, industrial demand, investment flows, and the US dollar. This calculator pulls a live spot figure so your melt value reflects the current market rather than a stale number.

From Scrap to .999 Bars: The Refining Process

Melt value assumes your silver can be turned back into pure bullion, and refiners do exactly that. In brief: incoming scrap is sorted and weighed, then melted into a homogeneous bar and assayed (a small sample is tested, usually by fire assay or XRF, to measure exact silver content). The melted metal is then refined - commonly by electrolysis or chemical methods - to strip out copper and other base metals, producing .999 fine silver that is cast into new bars. Because this costs money, the refiner keeps a slice of the value.

Net Melt Value After Refining Fees

The realistic number you can sell for is:

Net melt value = (spot x purity x weight) - refining and assay fees

Typical refiner fees run 2-5% of the metal value, sometimes plus a flat assay/lot charge on small batches. On a $1,000 melt-value lot, expect to net roughly $950-980 from a competitive refiner. Smaller lots lose a larger percentage to flat fees, which is why pooling silver before selling pays off.

When Melt Value Does NOT Apply

Some items are worth more whole than melted. Do not scrap these without an appraisal:

  • Key-date and rare coins - numismatic value can dwarf melt.
  • Antique hallmarked pieces - identifiable maker's marks and age can add collector value.
  • Designer or branded jewelry - names like Tiffany or Georg Jensen carry resale premiums.
  • Complete, named flatware patterns - a full set in a desirable pattern sells above melt.

Silver Melt Value FAQ

What is silver melt value?

It is the value of the pure silver contained in an item, calculated as weight x purity x spot price. It ignores craftsmanship and brand and counts only the recoverable metal.

How is melt value calculated?

Multiply the item's weight in troy ounces by its purity (e.g. 0.925 for sterling) and by the current silver spot price. For grams, divide grams by 31.1035 first, or use the calculator above.

Is melt value the same as scrap value?

Almost. Melt value is the full metal value at spot. Scrap value is what a buyer actually pays - melt value minus a refining margin, usually 88-97% of melt from a refiner or 70-90% from a shop.

What is the difference between melt value and spot price?

Spot price is the market price for one troy ounce of pure (.999) silver. Melt value is what your specific item is worth based on its actual weight and purity - always spot price scaled down by the item's purity.

Why is my silver worth less than I paid?

Retail price includes manufacturing and markup that you cannot recover. When you sell, you get melt value (plus any collector premium), not the store price. The metal content, not the original price tag, sets resale worth.

What are typical silver refining fees?

Usually 2-5% of the metal value, sometimes with a flat assay or lot charge on small batches. Larger lots keep a higher percentage because flat fees are spread across more metal.
