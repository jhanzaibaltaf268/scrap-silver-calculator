What Is .900 Coin Silver?

.900 silver (also called "coin silver") contains 90% pure silver and 10% copper. It is most commonly found in pre-1965 United States coins — dimes, quarters, half dollars, and dollar coins minted before the Coinage Act of 1965 removed silver from circulation. Some older American silver flatware and hollowware is also stamped ".900" or "coin silver."

The 10% copper alloy makes coin silver harder and more durable than sterling (.925), which is why it survived decades of circulation. To calculate the value of .900 silver, multiply weight in troy ounces by 0.900 by today's spot price.

Formula: melt value = (weight in grams ÷ 31.1035) × 0.900 × spot price

How to Identify .900 Coin Silver

  • Hallmarks on flatware: COIN, COIN SILVER, .900, or 900/1000 stamped on the back of the piece
  • Pre-1965 US coins: Dimes, quarters, half dollars, and silver dollars dated 1964 and earlier carry no hallmark — they are identified by date and denomination
  • Magnet test: Silver is not magnetic; if a magnet sticks to your piece, it is not solid silver
  • Acid test: For flatware of unknown origin, an acid test kit will confirm silver content; reputable dealers offer this service
  • Post-1965 US coins are clad: Quarters and dimes minted 1965 and later contain no silver — always check the date before calculating
