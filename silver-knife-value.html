How Much Is a Silver Knife Worth?

Valuing a silver knife is more complex than other flatware because of how they are constructed. Most "silver" dinner and butter knives consist of a hollow sterling silver handle attached to a stainless steel blade. To find the melt value, you must only calculate the weight of the silver handle, not the entire knife.

⚖️ The "Hollow Handle" Rule

Most silver knife handles are filled with a heavy cement, pitch, or resin to keep the blade secure and provide a balanced weight. This filler is not silver. A standard dinner knife weighing 75g may only contain **15–20g** of actual sterling silver.

Typical Silver Knife Weight Estimations

If you aren't prepared to break the blade off the handle, use these industry-standard estimates for silver content:

  • Modern Dinner Knife: 15 – 18 grams of silver
  • Victorian/Large Dinner Knife: 20 – 25 grams of silver
  • Butter Spreader (Hollow Handle): 10 – 12 grams of silver
  • Solid Silver Flat Handle Spreader: Full weight (usually 25–40g)

How to Identify Your Silver Knife

Look at the handle for the "Sterling" or "925" stamp. The blade will almost always be marked "Stainless" or "Stainless Steel"—this part has zero silver value. If the handle says "EPNS" or "Silver Plate," the item is plated and contains only a microscopic amount of silver.

Using the Knife Value Calculator

To get the most accurate result, enter only the estimated weight of the silver handle into our calculator. If you have a set of 12 knives, a good rule of thumb for scrap value is to assume approximately 0.5 to 0.6 troy ounces of pure silver per dinner knife handle.
