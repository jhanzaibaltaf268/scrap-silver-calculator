What Is Gold Melt Value?

Gold melt value is the intrinsic worth of the pure gold content in any item — what a refiner would pay for it based on weight and purity. Unlike silver, gold has a much higher per-gram value, making even small items worth significant amounts.

Understanding Gold Purity

Gold jewelry and items come in different purity levels:

  • 24K Fine (99.9% pure) — Bullion bars, coins, investment gold — rarely used in jewelry (too soft)
  • 22K (91.7% pure) — High-quality jewelry, especially in Middle Eastern and Indian gold
  • 18K (75% pure) — Fine jewelry standard in US/EU — excellent balance of purity and durability
  • 14K (58.3% pure) — Most common US gold jewelry — durable and affordable
  • 10K (41.7% pure) — Budget jewelry, wedding rings — lowest acceptable standard for "gold" in US

Where to Look for Hallmarks

Find purity stamps on:

  • Inside ring bands
  • Behind necklace clasps
  • Bottom of watch cases
  • Brooches and pins (usually etched on back)
  • Bracelets (often on the clasp or end link)

Common hallmarks: 999 (24K), 917 (22K), 750 (18K), 585 (14K), 417 (10K), or just "GOLD" / "10K" / "14K" etc.

Why Gold Sells Faster Than Silver

Gold has distinct advantages in the scrap market:

  • Higher per-gram value = smaller minimum acceptable amounts
  • More recognizable to buyers (jewelry, wedding rings, coins)
  • Less expertise needed to verify authenticity
  • Stable demand from refiners, jewelers, and collectors

How Much Will a Dealer Pay?

Dealers typically pay 80–95% of melt value depending on buyer type:

  • Online refineries: 90–98% of melt (best option for larger amounts)
  • Local jewelers: 75–85% of melt (convenient, immediate payment)
  • Pawn shops: 50–70% of melt (avoid — worst rates)
  • Coin dealers: 85–95% of melt (if coins have no numismatic premium)
