Troy Ounce vs Regular Ounce: The Critical Difference for Silver

Every silver price quoted per ounce uses the troy ounce — not the standard (avoirdupois) ounce used for everyday items. These two units differ by almost 10%, and confusing them is a costly mistake when valuing silver.

⚖️ Troy Ounce — The Silver Standard

31.1035 g = $—
Used for: All precious metals worldwide
  • 1 troy lb = 12 troy oz (not 16)
  • Standard for gold, silver, platinum globally
  • All COMEX and LBMA pricing uses troy oz
  • 1 troy oz = 1.09714 avoirdupois oz

🧴 Standard Ounce (Avoirdupois)

28.3495 g = $—
Used for: Everyday items, food, packages
  • 1 standard lb = 16 oz
  • Used for groceries, shipping, body weight
  • Never used in precious metals pricing
  • 1 avdp oz = 0.91146 troy oz

The bottom line: When a coin shop or refiner quotes silver at $—/oz, they mean per troy ounce. If you calculate with 28.35g instead of 31.1035g, you undervalue every ounce by 9.7%. Our scrap calculator always uses the correct troy ounce, so you'll never make this error.

How to Calculate Silver Value Using Today's Per-Ounce Price

Four steps from weighing your silver to an accurate melt value using today's live spot price.

1

Weigh in Grams

Use a digital scale accurate to at least 0.1g. Weigh each item separately. Kitchen scales work for large pieces; a jewelry scale is better for rings and small items.

Example: Sterling spoon = 58.4g
2

Convert to Troy Ounces

Divide grams by 31.1035. This is the critical step — using 28.35 (the standard oz) will produce a wrong answer and undervalue your silver by nearly 10%.

58.4 ÷ 31.1035 = 1.878 troy oz
3

Apply the Purity Factor

Multiply by the purity decimal: 0.999 for fine silver, 0.925 for sterling, 0.900 for US coin silver, or 0.800 for European silver.

1.878 × 0.925 = 1.737 pure troy oz
4

Multiply by Today's Price

Silver Price Per Ounce: Frequently Asked Questions

Today's silver price per troy ounce is displayed live at the top of this page, refreshed every hour from global commodity exchanges including COMEX. It reflects the spot market price for 999 fine silver in US dollars — the same reference price used by refineries, bullion dealers, and commodity traders worldwide.

Silver is always priced in troy ounces (31.1035g), never in standard avoirdupois ounces (28.3495g). The troy system has been the global standard for precious metals since the Middle Ages. Using the wrong ounce type creates a 9.7% calculation error — always divide by 31.1035 when converting silver price from ounces to grams.

Divide the troy ounce spot price by 31.1035 to get the price per gram of pure 999 silver. For purity-adjusted prices, multiply the result by the purity decimal (0.925 for sterling, 0.900 for coin silver, etc.). Our unit conversion table above shows all values live at today's spot rate.

The troy system dates to medieval Troyes, France — a major trading hub where European merchants standardized precious metal weights long before the metric system existed. When commodity exchanges formalized in the 19th and 20th centuries, they inherited this convention. The troy ounce remains the global de facto standard for gold, silver, and platinum pricing to this day.

The nominal all-time high was $49.51 per troy ounce on April 25, 2011, driven by speculative investment demand. In real inflation-adjusted terms, the 1980 peak (around $48/oz) equates to over $170 in 2026 dollars, making it the true historical record. The 2011 high remains the benchmark for modern nominal pricing discussions.

There are exactly 31.1035 grams in one troy ounce of silver. This is the conversion factor used by every commodity exchange, refinery, and silver dealer worldwide. A 1 oz American Silver Eagle coin, for example, contains exactly 31.1035g of 0.999 fine silver.
