How Much is a Silver Dime Worth?

A "Silver Dime" typically refers to the 90% silver coins minted by the United States before 1965. These include the Roosevelt Dime (1946–1964) and the iconic Mercury Dime (1916–1945). Because of their small size and fixed silver content, dimes are the most common entry point for investors starting a "junk silver" collection.

💰 Small Unit Advantage

Silver dimes are often considered the most practical form of silver for bartering or small transactions. While a 100oz bar is worth thousands, a single silver dime represents a much smaller, more spendable unit of wealth. 14 dimes contain approximately one troy ounce of pure silver.

Technical Specifications of a Silver Dime

Whether you have a Mercury or a Roosevelt dime, the physical silver specs are identical:

  • Total Weight: 2.50 grams
  • Composition: 90% Silver, 10% Copper
  • Pure Silver Weight: 2.25 grams (0.07234 troy ounces)

If you have a large bag of silver dimes, you can estimate the total weight easily: $1.00 face value (10 dimes) always contains approximately 0.723 troy ounces of silver bullion.

Identifying Silver Dimes (Pre-1965)

Checking the date is the easiest way to identify a silver dime: any dime dated **1964 or earlier** is 90% silver. However, you can also check the edge of the coin. A silver dime will have a solid white/silver edge, whereas a modern clad dime (1965–present) will show a distinct copper-colored stripe. This "edge test" is the industry standard for quickly sorting bulk coins.

Melt Value vs. Collectible Value

While most Roosevelt dimes are valued strictly for their silver melt price, some **Mercury Dimes** (especially the 1916-D or 1921 dates) carry a significant numismatic premium.
