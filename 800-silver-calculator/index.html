What Is .800 Silver?

.800 silver contains 80% pure silver and 20% other metals, primarily copper. That 20% copper alloy is not incidental — it gives .800 silver a notably harder physical character than sterling, making it well-suited for flatware and serving pieces that endure daily use. The .800 standard is the minimum silver content recognized by most European national assay offices, the floor below which an item cannot legally be sold as silver in most of the continent.

This standard was formally codified in Germany through the Silver Ordinance of 1884, alongside the .835 standard. The choice was deliberate: .800 was designated for everyday domestic silver where cost efficiency mattered more than maximum silver content. .835 was the standard for higher-end pieces; .800 served the broader market. The distinction still matters today when you are selling, since .800 and .835 carry different melt values.

.800 silver appears across a remarkably wide geographic range: German silverware produced between 1880 and the mid-twentieth century, Dutch domestic silver from the nineteenth century, Austrian silver, Swiss cantonal silver, Middle Eastern decorative silver (Egyptian and Turkish pieces), and some South American colonial and post-colonial silverware. German output alone makes .800 the single most common European silver standard encountered by sellers in North America today — German immigrant households brought enormous quantities of .800 flatware and serving pieces to the United States and Canada.

How to Identify .800 Silver — Hallmarks by Country

The hallmark is the only reliable non-destructive confirmation of silver purity. A magnet test tells you whether an item is magnetic (and therefore not silver), but it cannot distinguish .800 from .925 or .835. For European silver, reading the stamp is everything.

Germany — the primary source of .800 silver

Post-1888 German .800 silver shows the number "800" alongside a crescent moon (☽) pointing left and a crown (♛). This crescent-and-crown combination is the most recognized European silver mark outside British sterling, and its presence is definitive confirmation of German .800 silver. Pre-1888 German pieces may show "800" with an imperial eagle (Reichsadler) rather than the crescent and crown. East German pieces produced between 1949 and 1990 show "800" without the crown — the crown mark was dropped in the German Democratic Republic after partition.

Netherlands

"800" appears alongside a lion holding arrows — the Dutch lion passant. Dutch pieces may also carry a city mark identifying the assay office of origin. The combination of the lion mark and "800" is the key identifier.

Austria

"800" appears with the Austrian national eagle or, on pieces assayed after 1866, the Dianakopf — Diana's head, a profile portrait used as the Austrian assay mark for silver. The Dianakopf is one of the more elegant assay marks in European silver history and is a reliable indicator of Austrian origin.

Switzerland

Swiss .800 silver varies by canton. Look for "800" with cantonal animal marks — a bear for Bern, a bull for Uri, an eagle for various other cantons. Swiss pieces are less frequently encountered by North American sellers but do appear in estate sales.

Middle East

Egyptian silver is very frequently marked "800" and is one of the more common .800 silver types encountered in the United States due to import volume during the twentieth century. Turkish Ottoman-era silver uses tughra marks — the sultan's monogram — alongside fineness numerals. Post-Ottoman Turkish silver uses the fineness number with a crescent and star mark.

South America

Colonial and post-colonial pieces from Peru, Bolivia, Argentina, and other silver-producing regions may show "800" alongside regional assay marks or maker's marks. These pieces vary considerably and often require specialist identification.

How to find the mark: Check the back of flatware handles at the base, the rim underside of bowls and pitchers, the underside of serving pieces, and the clasp or inner ring of jewelry. Marks are typically 1–3mm in size. A 10× loupe is essential — the number "800" will be clearly stamped when present, but is too small to read reliably with the naked eye. Genuine marks are sharp and struck into the metal; photocopied or painted marks are a fraud indicator.

What Dealers Pay for .800 Silver and Where to Sell

.800 silver is fully accepted by most silver dealers, coin dealers, and refiners operating in North America and Europe. The metal has genuine market liquidity — refiners treat .800 and .925 silver identically once assayed, paying for the actual fine silver content regardless of the alloy composition of the remainder. The main practical challenge is not the silver's value but finding buyers who correctly identify .800 and pay accordingly.

Some non-specialist buyers — pawn shops, general antique dealers, and inexperienced coin dealers — default to paying lower rates when they encounter European marks unfamiliar to them. The crescent-and-crown German hallmark is well-known in the silver trade, but it is worth presenting your purity explicitly: "This is .800 silver with the crescent-and-crown German hallmark — the melt value at today's spot price is $X." That statement closes the information gap and prevents the buyer from using uncertainty as leverage.

Payout rates across buyer types follow the same pattern as for sterling and other silver grades: local coin and silver dealers typically pay 80–90% of melt value for same-day cash; online bullion buyers pay 85–92%; professional refiners pay 88–95% for lots of 500 grams or more of pure silver content. For .800 silver, 500 grams of pure silver content means 625 grams of actual .800 material — a realistic lot size for anyone selling a flatware service.

One important exception: antique .800 pieces in excellent condition — fine German or Dutch silverware with intact chasing, engraving, or known maker's marks — may be worth significantly more than melt. German auction houses including Dorotheum Vienna and Hampel Munich actively handle quality .800 pieces at 1.5 to 4× melt for exceptional craftsmanship. If a piece has ornate decoration, maker's marks you can look up, or obvious age, consult a specialist before scrapping. Melt value is always a floor, never a ceiling.
