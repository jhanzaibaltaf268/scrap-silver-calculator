How to Calculate Scrap Silver Value

Calculating the melt value of your scrap silver follows a straightforward formula. Understanding this process helps you verify calculator results and negotiate confidently with buyers.

The Melt Value Formula

Melt Value = (Weight in Troy Ounces) × (Purity %) × (Silver Spot Price per Troy Ounce)
Important: Precious metals use troy ounces, not standard avoirdupois ounces. One troy ounce equals approximately 31.1035 grams, while a standard ounce is only 28.3495 grams. Using the wrong unit can significantly undervalue your silver.

Step-by-Step Calculation Example

A sterling silver bracelet weighing 50 grams, with silver at $30.00 per troy ounce:

  1. Convert grams to troy ounces: 50 ÷ 31.1035 = 1.608 troy ounces
  2. Apply purity: 1.608 × 0.925 (92.5% sterling) = 1.487 troy ounces of pure silver
  3. Calculate melt value: 1.487 × $30.00 = $44.61

Silver Scrap Buy and Sell Value: What Dealers Pay vs. Melt Value

A critical reality of the scrap silver market is that no dealer pays 100% of melt value. Dealers must cover refining costs, market volatility risk, and operating margins.

Typical Dealer Payout Structure

Seller TypeExpected Payout RangeNotes
Walk-in pawn shops50%–70% of meltQuick cash but lowest returns
Local coin dealers80%–90% of meltBetter for coins with potential numismatic value
Online bullion buyers85%–95% of meltCompetitive rates, requires shipping and waiting
Professional refiners90%–98% of meltBest for large volumes, often B2B only
Direct peer sales95%–100%+ of meltHighest potential but requires time and trust

Why the gap? Refiners extract pure silver from scrap through smelting and chemical processes. These operations consume energy, labor, and equipment. Additionally, silver prices fluctuate constantly — dealers hedge against drops between purchase and resale.

How Much Is Silver Worth? Current Market Context

Silver pricing operates on global commodity markets, with the spot price representing the immediate-delivery value for one troy ounce of .999 fine silver.

Factors Influencing Silver Spot Prices

🏭

Industrial Demand

Solar panels, electronics, medical equipment, and batteries consume roughly 50% of annual silver production.

📈

⛏️

Precious metals historically serve as inflation hedges.

Frequently Asked Questions

Melt value is the intrinsic worth of a precious-metal item based solely on its metal content, ignoring any collectible, artistic, or numismatic premium. For scrap silver, it represents the raw material value if the item were melted and refined to pure silver.

Melt value is calculated by multiplying the item's weight (in troy ounces) by its purity percentage (e.g., 0.925 for sterling) by the current spot price of silver per troy ounce. Always use troy ounces, not standard ounces, for precious metals.

Dealers pay less than melt value to cover refining costs, business overhead, and market risk. Silver prices can drop between purchase and resale. Payout percentages vary by buyer type, with refiners offering the highest percentages (often 90%+) for bulk scrap.

Our calculator accepts grams, pennyweight (dwt), standard ounces, and troy ounces. For accuracy, we recommend weighing scrap silver in grams or troy ounces. Remember: 1 troy ounce = 31.1035 grams = 20 pennyweight.

A typical sterling silver ring weighing 5 grams contains 4.625 grams of pure silver (5 g × 0.925). At $30.00/oz spot, that's approximately $4.46 in melt value. A dealer might offer $3.50–$4.20 depending on their payout rate.

Junk silver refers to pre-1965 US coins with 90% silver content (dimes, quarters, half dollars, and dollars) that have no collectible value beyond their metal content. Despite the name, junk silver is highly liquid among investors and dealers.

A pre-1965 Washington quarter weighs 6.25 grams and contains 90% silver. That's 5.625 grams (0.181 troy ounces) of pure silver per coin. At $30.00/oz, each quarter has approximately $5.43 in melt value.

In the United States, it is currently legal to melt silver coins for their metal content. However, melting pennies and nickels for their copper and nickel content is prohibited under 31 CFR § 82.1 due to commodity value exceeding face value. Laws vary by country.

Interesting Facts About Silver

Best Conductor

Silver has the highest electrical and thermal conductivity of all metals, making it irreplaceable in electronics and solar technology.

🏛️

Ancient Currency

Silver has served as money for over 4,000 years — longer than any other form of currency.

⚕️

Medical Properties

Silver's antimicrobial properties make it valuable in wound dressings and water purification.

📷

Photographic Legacy

Before digital cameras, photography consumed nearly 25% of annual silver production.

📉

Above-Ground Scarcity

Despite being more abundant than gold in the earth's crust, most mined silver is consumed industrially, leaving relatively little investment-grade stockpile.
