What Is 958 Britannia Silver?

Britannia silver contains 95.8% pure silver and 4.2% copper — the second purest silver standard in the world after .999 fine silver. Its history is uniquely English and tied directly to protecting the national currency. In 1696, English silversmiths had been systematically melting silver coins to use as raw material for making plate. The practice was visibly depleting the national coinage and destabilizing commerce at a critical moment in English economic history.

Parliament responded with the Plate Offences Act of 1696 and introduced the Britannia standard in 1697. The new standard was deliberately set at .958 — higher than the .925 sterling standard that coins were made to. This made coin silver too low in purity to legally produce Britannia-standard plate, so melting coins for silversmithing became commercially unviable overnight. It was a legislative solution to a monetary crisis disguised as a hallmarking reform.

The Britannia standard was mandatory for all English silversmithing from 1697 until 1720, when sterling (.925) was reinstated as an option alongside Britannia — which has remained legal but optional ever since. This 23-year mandatory period produced some of the finest English silver ever made. Pieces by documented makers such as Paul de Lamerie, Simon Pantin, Anthony Nelme, and David Willaume are museum-quality objects that routinely sell for multiples of their melt value at major auction houses. If you have Britannia period silver, its historical significance matters far more than its silver content.

Today, Britannia silver (.958) is voluntarily produced by some UK silversmiths for specialty and prestige items, where the higher purity is a mark of quality above the sterling standard. One important distinction: the Royal Mint's Britannia bullion coin series (1997–present) is .999 fine silver — not .958 Britannia standard — despite carrying the Britannia image. The coin and the antique standard share only the name and the figure. Always check the fineness stamp to confirm which you have before calculating any melt value.

Britannia Silver Hallmarks — How to Identify .958 Silver

Britannia silver has one of the most visually distinctive hallmark systems in English silver. The primary Britannia hallmark is a seated female figure: Britannia herself, shown holding a trident in her right hand and a shield — bearing the cross of St. George — against her left side. This figure is approximately 4–6mm tall on typical pieces. Under a 10x loupe, the trident, shield, and draped robes are clearly visible and rendered with fine detail. If you can see a seated woman with a trident and shield, you have Britannia silver.

On pieces made during the mandatory Britannia period (1697–1720), the Britannia figure appears alongside a lion's head erased — a lion's head facing left with a jagged, roughly cut neck, as if severed rather than cleanly cut. This "erased" neck distinguishes it immediately from the lion passant (a walking lion, shown complete) used on sterling silver. Parliament chose this deliberately: the two marks were designed to be visually different at a glance, signaling the higher standard and preventing confusion between the two systems. If you see a walking lion, that is sterling. If you see a severed lion's head, that is Britannia.

Post-1720 Britannia silver — made under the optional standard — carries the Britannia figure alongside the standard UK assay office marks: a date letter, an assay office mark (the leopard's head for London, anchor for Birmingham, castle for Edinburgh), and the maker's mark punch. Some modern pieces are simply marked "958" or "958 Britannia" alongside the maker's mark without the traditional figure.

Where to find the marks: on flatware, check the handle backs near the bowl or tine end; on hollowware such as teapots and jugs, check undersides and rims; on smaller items, check clasps, hinges, and the inside of lids. The date letter system used by British assay offices allows precise dating to the specific year of assay — Jackson's Hallmarks (the standard reference) and the Silver Society contain full date letter tables for all offices and periods.

Important: Royal Mint Britannia bullion coins (1997–present) are stamped .999 or 999 — NOT .958. They bear the Britannia image but are not Britannia standard silver. Check the fineness stamp before calculating melt value.

Selling .958 Britannia Silver — Two Scenarios

Britannia silver divides into two completely different selling situations. Identifying which applies before approaching any buyer is essential — the appropriate action differs dramatically between them.

Scenario 1 — Antique Britannia period silver (pre-1720)

Do not scrap without specialist appraisal. Even ordinary domestic pieces from the mandatory Britannia period (1697–1720) carry significant historical value that almost always exceeds melt. Major salerooms regularly achieve hammer prices 3–20 times melt value for Britannia period silver at their silver and objects of vertu sales. Pieces by documented makers — Paul de Lamerie, Simon Pantin, Anthony Nelme, David Willaume — can sell for 50–200 times melt value when condition, provenance, and maker's reputation align. Contact Christie's London silver department or the Silver Society (silversociety.org) for guidance before approaching any buyer. A specialist opinion costs nothing compared to the difference between melt value and proper sale value.

Scenario 2 — Modern .958 specialty pieces (post-1950)

These are quality modern silver produced voluntarily at the higher Britannia standard. They sell to standard silver dealers and refiners at the .958 rate — typically 85–93% of melt value. Some contemporary British silversmiths with strong reputations command collector premiums above melt for signed modern pieces, but this is maker-dependent rather than standard-dependent. Identify the piece as .958, not .925, when approaching any buyer — the per-gram difference is real and compounds meaningfully on larger pieces.
