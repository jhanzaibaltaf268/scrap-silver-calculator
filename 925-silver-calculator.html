How to Calculate the Scrap Value of 925 Silver Before You Sell

If you have a drawer of old 925 rings, broken chains, or single earrings, this page is built to answer one question: how much cash is it actually worth as scrap? The short version is that scrap value is based on the silver content, not the jewelry's original retail price - and 925 means 92.5% of the weight is sellable silver. Weigh the piece, multiply by 0.925, multiply by the spot price, and you have your melt value. The calculator above does this instantly; the sections below explain how to identify real 925, spot fakes, and know what a buyer should pay.

What Does 925 Mean?

925 means 925 parts silver per 1,000 - that is 92.5% pure silver, with the remaining 7.5% usually copper. This alloy is called sterling silver. The copper is added on purpose: pure silver is too soft to hold a setting or survive daily wear, while the 7.5% copper makes sterling hard enough for rings, chains, bracelets, and flatware while keeping nearly all of silver's brightness.

Why 925 Became the Sterling Standard

The 92.5% standard dates back to medieval England. By the 1300s the English Crown had fixed the silver content of coinage and wares, and the London assay office (founded 1300 under Edward I) enforced it with hallmarks. The word "sterling" itself is tied to the early English silver penny. Over centuries 92.5% became the internationally recognized benchmark for quality silver - high enough purity to be valuable, durable enough to be useful - and it remains the global standard for jewelry today.

How to Identify Genuine 925 Silver

Real sterling is almost always marked. Look for a "925," ".925," "STERLING," "STER," or "STG" stamp. Where to find it depends on the item:

  • Rings: on the inside of the band.
  • Chains and necklaces: on the clasp or on a small tag soldered next to the clasp.
  • Bracelets: on the clasp, end link, or a tag.
  • Earrings: on the post or the back of the setting (often tiny - use a loupe).
  • Flatware and holloware: on the underside of the handle or base.

Quick at-home checks beyond the stamp: a strong magnet should not attract silver (sterling is non-magnetic, so if it sticks, it is plated steel); a soft cloth rubbed on real silver may show a light gray mark, while plated pieces often do not behave the same; and real silver has a bright, clear "ring" when tapped. None of these is conclusive on its own - an acid test kit or an XRF scan at a coin shop is the definitive answer.

A 925 stamp is a claim, not a guarantee

Stamping is cheap and unregulated in many countries. Treat the mark as a starting point. If a piece is heavy, valuable, or you are buying it, confirm with an acid test or XRF scan before paying sterling prices.

Fake 925 Stamps: What to Watch For

Counterfeit and overstated "925" marks are common, especially from unregulated import channels. Warning signs include:

  • "Thai silver" or "Bali 925" that tests below 92.5% - some is genuine sterling, but a portion is lower-grade alloy stamped 925 anyway.
  • Cheap import jewelry stamped 925 that is actually plated brass or nickel silver (which contains no silver at all). If a magnet grabs it, it is not sterling.
  • "925" plus a separate "GF" or "GP" mark - GF means gold-filled and GP means gold-plated, signs the piece is a base-metal core.
  • Green skin discoloration after wear, which points to high copper or brass content rather than true sterling.

Calculating 925 Scrap Value: The Formula

The melt value of sterling is:

Value = weight in troy ounces x 0.925 x spot price

If your scale reads grams, first divide grams by 31.1035 to get troy ounces, or just enter grams directly into the calculator above. Using a sample spot price of $32.00 per troy ounce:

  • 5 g sterling ring: (5 / 31.1035) x 0.925 x $32.00 = $4.76 melt value.
  • 20 g sterling chain: (20 / 31.1035) x 0.925 x $32.00 = $19.04.
  • 1 troy ounce of sterling: 1 x 0.925 x $32.00 = $29.60.
  • 100 g of mixed sterling scrap: (100 / 31.1035) x 0.925 x $32.00 = $95.20.

What Refiners and Jewelers Actually Pay for 925 Scrap

Melt value is the ceiling, not the offer. Every buyer keeps a margin to cover refining, assay, and profit. Typical real-world payouts on sterling scrap:

  • Bulk refiners (by mail, larger lots): 88-95% of melt after fees.
  • Local coin or jewelry shops: 70-85% of melt for walk-in scrap.
  • Pawn shops: often 50-70% of melt.

So if 100 g of sterling has a $95.20 melt value, expect roughly $67-81 at a shop, $84-90 from a good refiner, and as little as $48-67 at a pawn counter. Heavier, simpler pieces (chains, bangles) fetch the best rates; intricate or gemstone-set pieces pay less because the labor adds no scrap value. Always price your lot at melt first so you can recognize a fair offer.

925 Jewelry Buying and Weighing Guide by Type

Rough weights help you estimate before you even reach a scale:

  • Rings: typically 2-8 g each (chunky statement rings can hit 15 g+).
  • Chains: light chains 3-10 g; heavy curb or rope chains 20-60 g.
  • Bracelets: 10-40 g depending on style.
  • Earrings: 1-5 g per pair (studs are tiny; hoops more).
  • Pendants: 2-15 g.

Remember to subtract non-silver parts - gemstones, glue, steel ear wires, and clasp springs all add weight that is not silver. Learn more: What Does 925 Mean on Silver?

925 Silver FAQ

Is 925 silver real silver?

Yes. 925 (sterling) is genuine silver - 92.5% pure, alloyed with 7.5% copper for strength. It is the most common form of real silver jewelry sold worldwide.

What is 925 silver worth per gram?

About 92.5% of the per-gram spot price of silver. At a $32 troy-ounce spot price, one gram of pure silver is roughly $1.029, so one gram of 925 sterling holds about $0.952 of silver melt value.

What is the difference between 925 and 999 silver?

925 is 92.5% pure (sterling, with copper added for durability) and 999 is 99.9% pure (fine silver, used for bullion). Fine silver has more metal value per gram but is too soft for jewelry; sterling is harder and used for rings, chains, and flatware.

How can I tell if my 925 stamp is fake?

Test it. A magnet should not stick to real silver; an acid test kit or an XRF scan at a coin shop gives a definitive purity reading. Green skin marks, a "925 GP/GF" combo stamp, or a magnetic response all suggest a plated or base-metal fake.

How much will a dealer pay for my 925 jewelry?

Usually 70-85% of melt value at a local shop, up to 88-95% from a bulk refiner, and as low as 50-70% at a pawn shop. Calculate the melt value first so you know your floor before negotiating.

Does 925 silver tarnish?

Yes. The copper in sterling reacts with air and sulfur to form a dark tarnish over time. Tarnish is surface-only and does not reduce the silver content or melt value - it polishes off easily, and refiners remelt regardless of surface condition.
