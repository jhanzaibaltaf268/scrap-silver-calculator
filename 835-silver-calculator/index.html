What Is 835 Silver? (Was Ist 835 Silber?)

.835 silver contains 83.5% pure silver and 16.5% other metals, primarily copper. This specific purity level is not arbitrary — it was one of two silver standards formally codified in Germany by the Silver Ordinance of 1884, the other being .800. Within the German system, .835 represented the higher quality tier: better than .800 silver for quality flatware and decorative items, while remaining below the British sterling .925 standard preferred by luxury silversmiths working for the export market.

The .835 standard spread across northern Europe because several Scandinavian countries adopted it as their national silver standard. Sweden, Norway, Denmark, and Finland all worked primarily in .835 through the 19th and early 20th centuries. Swedish silversmiths produced vast quantities of .835 flatware, hollowware, and decorative silver that are now held in family collections across Sweden. This explains why the Swedish query "835 silver värde" (835 silver value) is so common today — generations of Swedes have inherited .835 silverware from grandparents and great-grandparents and seek its current melt value. The Netherlands also used .835 for certain production periods, and Belgium applied it to specific wares.

In terms of value, .835 silver sits precisely between .800 and .925 on the purity scale. At today's silver spot price, .835 is worth $— more per gram than .800 silver, and $— less per gram than sterling .925. On a 1 kilogram lot, the difference between .835 and .800 silver is $— — and the difference between .835 and sterling is $—. These are significant amounts that make it worth confirming the exact purity hallmark before accepting any offer from a dealer.

The practical character of .835 silver is similar to other silver alloys in this purity range: it tarnishes readily in air due to the copper content, it is workable for smithing and stamping, and it holds detail well in cast and pressed flatware. The alloy was ideal for the mass production of domestic silverware that characterized 19th-century German and Scandinavian silver manufacturing — strong enough for daily flatware use, pure enough to carry genuine silver value, and cost-competitive against the higher .925 standard.

.835 Silver Hallmarks — Country by Country

Identifying .835 silver correctly before selling is essential — confusing it with .800 silver costs money, and confirming it as .835 rather than .925 sterling prevents a buyer from underpaying. Each country that used the .835 standard developed its own hallmarking system, but the number "835" always appears clearly stamped on genuine .835 pieces. Use a 10x loupe: marks are typically 1–4mm and require magnification to read reliably.

Germany — The Most Common Source

German .835 silver is the most frequently encountered .835 in Western markets. After 1888, German pieces carry the number "835" alongside two iconic symbols: a crescent moon (☽) and a crown (♛). This crescent-and-crown system is the same one used for German .800 silver — the number differentiates the two standards. Pre-1888 German pieces may show the imperial eagle instead of the crown alongside the "835" mark. East German pieces produced between 1949 and 1990 typically show "835" without the crown, as the GDR discontinued the imperial crown symbol. Maker's marks appear alongside these assay marks and can be researched in Rosenberg's Goldschmiedemerken, the standard reference for German goldsmith marks.

Sweden — Exceptionally Well-Documented

Swedish .835 silver hallmarking is among the best-documented in the world. Legal hallmarking was required from 1752, and Swedish assay records are exceptionally well-preserved. A Swedish .835 piece typically shows: the number "835," the letter "S" in a rectangle (the silver mark), a two-letter date code (which can identify the exact year of manufacture), and a city letter — S for Stockholm, G for Gothenburg, M for Malmö. This system means that virtually every Swedish .835 piece can be dated to the specific year and city of assay. Swedish maker's marks are documented in the Nationalencyklopedins silvermarks database, which is accessible online for research.

Norway, Denmark, and Finland

Norwegian .835 silver shows the number "835" alongside national assay marks and city letters — Oslo is marked O, Bergen is B. Finnish .835 uses "835" with Finnish assay marks and often a crown-plus-date system. Danish .835 is less common than Danish .830 (a slightly different national standard) but appears on pieces from certain periods alongside the three towers mark of Copenhagen assay.

Netherlands and Belgium

Dutch .835 silver is rarer than Dutch .800 — look for "835" alongside the Dutch assay lion mark. Belgian .835 uses lion-based marks alongside the "835" number. Both Dutch and Belgian .835 pieces are less commonly encountered in the current resale market than German or Scandinavian pieces.

Where to Find the Marks

On flatware: the back of the handle near the base, or the back of the bowl near where it joins the handle. On hollowware (pitchers, sugar bowls, tea pots): the underside or the rim. On jewelry: clasps, posts, and the inner surfaces of bands. Always check multiple locations — on older pieces, marks can be worn and a secondary mark may be more legible than the primary one.

Where to Sell .835 Silver — Best Buyers and Payouts

.835 silver is a recognized standard in the international silver market, and most professional buyers will accept it readily when the hallmarks are clear. In the US, coin dealers and silver refiners who handle European silver regularly will identify the crescent-and-crown German hallmark immediately — no explanation required. Payouts from US buyers typically fall in the 80–95% range depending on buyer type and lot size: local coin dealers pay 80–90% for same-day cash, online silver buyers pay 85–92%, and professional refiners pay 88–95% for lots of 500 grams or more.

In Scandinavia, the market for Swedish .835 silver is active and well-developed. Bukowskis auction house in Stockholm is the premier venue for quality Swedish silver, handling both inherited domestic pieces and collector-grade silverware. Stockholms Auktionsverk regularly processes .835 Swedish flatware and hollowware. Norwegian silver finds its natural buyers at Blomqvist Kunsthandel in Oslo, while Bruun Rasmussen in Denmark handles both Danish and Norwegian pieces. These Scandinavian auction houses are worth considering for any piece with a documented provenance or a notable maker's mark — they attract collectors willing to pay above melt for pieces with verifiable history.

For German .835, the major German auction houses — Dorotheum, Hampel, and Nagel — achieve above-melt results for quality signed German silverware. Before selling any German .835 piece through a refiner, check the maker's mark in Rosenberg's Goldschmiedemerken. Similarly, check Swedish pieces in the Nationalencyklopedins silvermarks database. A signed piece by a recognized master silversmith can be worth 2–5 times its melt value when sold through the right channel — a difference that can represent hundreds or thousands of dollars on a significant piece.
