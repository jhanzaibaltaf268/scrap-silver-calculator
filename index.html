The Complete Guide to Scrap Silver Valuation

1. How to Calculate the Value of Scrap Silver

A scrap silver calculator determines the intrinsic melt value of a silver item — not its retail, numismatic, or sentimental value. It answers one question: if you melted this item down to pure silver today, what would that metal be worth on the open market?

Melt Value = (Weight in Troy Oz × Purity %) × Spot Price Per Troy Oz

For example, a 100-gram sterling silver necklace (92.5% purity) at a silver spot price of $30.00/oz:

  • Convert grams to troy oz: 100 ÷ 31.1035 = 3.215 troy oz
  • Multiply by purity: 3.215 × 0.925 = 2.974 oz pure silver
  • Multiply by spot price: 2.974 × $30.00 = $89.21 melt value

Our calculator handles all of these conversions automatically with 4-decimal precision — the same methodology used by professional refineries and precious metals dealers worldwide.

What Affects Your Scrap Silver's Value?

  • Silver spot price: The global market price for 999 fine silver at that moment. It fluctuates throughout the trading day.
  • Purity / fineness: Higher purity = more silver per gram = higher value. 999 is worth more per gram than 925, which is worth more than 900.
  • Weight: Measured in troy ounces (31.1035 g each), not standard ounces. Using the wrong measurement is a very common and costly mistake.
  • Dealer spread: Dealers pay 70–95% of melt value. Refiners typically pay the most; pawn shops the least.

2. Sterling Silver vs Fine Silver vs Coin Silver — What's the Difference?

Not all silver is the same. The three most common types you'll encounter when scrapping are fine silver (999), sterling silver (925), and coin silver (900). Here's how they compare:

999 Fine Silver

The purest commercially available silver — 99.9% silver with trace impurities. Found in modern bullion coins (American Silver Eagles, Canadian Maple Leafs), bars, and investment-grade rounds. Commands the highest melt value per ounce and is the benchmark for all other purities.

925 Sterling Silver

The most common silver alloy — 92.5% silver alloyed with 7.5% copper (occasionally other metals) to increase hardness and durability. Sterling is used for jewelry, flatware, holloware, and many decorative objects. Look for "925", "STERLING", or "STG" stamps.

900 Coin Silver

A 90% silver / 10% copper alloy used in US coinage from the mid-1800s through 1964. Pre-1964 dimes, quarters, half dollars, and dollars are 90% silver. Also called "junk silver" — a misleading name, since these coins are popular with investors for their predictable silver content.

Other Purities You May Encounter

  • 958 Britannia: 95.8% silver. Required for Britannia Silver standard in the UK.
  • 835: 83.5% silver. Common in European jewelry and flatware (especially German and Scandinavian).
  • 800: 80% silver. Used extensively in continental European silverware through the early 20th century.
  • 720: 72% silver. Found in older Swiss and Austrian pieces.

Items marked EPNS (Electroplated Nickel Silver), "Silver Plate", or "Sheffield Plate" contain only a thin coating of silver over base metal. These have negligible scrap value — typically less than a few dollars regardless of weight.

How to Find and Read a Hallmark

On jewelry, look inside rings, on the clasp of necklaces or bracelets, and at the back of pendants. On flatware, check the back near the handle. Use a loupe (10x magnifier) — many hallmarks are tiny. If you can't find a stamp, the item may be silver-plated or from a period/country that didn't require marking.

4. Troy Ounces vs Regular Ounces — Why It Matters for Silver

One of the most common (and expensive) mistakes when calculating silver value is confusing troy ounces with standard (avoirdupois) ounces. These are two different units of measurement, and using the wrong one will give you incorrect results every time.

The Key Difference

  • 1 Troy Ounce (ozt) = 31.1035 grams — used for all precious metals (silver, gold, platinum)
  • 1 Standard Ounce (oz) = 28.3495 grams — used for everyday goods (food, groceries, shipping)

A troy ounce is approximately 10% heavier than a regular ounce. If silver is priced at $30.00 per troy ounce and you accidentally calculate using standard ounces, you'll underestimate the value by about 9.7% — a meaningful error on any significant quantity.

Other Units Used for Silver

  • Pennyweight (dwt): 1 troy oz = 20 pennyweights. Commonly used by jewelers. 1 dwt = 1.5552 grams.
  • Gram (g): The most precise and universally understood unit. Best for weighing jewelry and small items.
  • Kilogram (kg): Used for large quantities of scrap silver. 1 kg = 32.1507 troy ounces.
  • Tola: Traditional South Asian unit. 1 tola = 11.6638 grams = 0.375 troy oz. Still used in India, Pakistan, and parts of the Middle East.

Our calculator accepts all major weight units and converts automatically — just select the unit your scale uses.

5. How to Test If Your Silver Is Real

Before calculating a scrap value, you need to confirm that your item is actually silver and not silver-plated, nickel silver, or a base-metal lookalike. Here are five methods from easiest to most accurate:

  1. Look for a Hallmark: The fastest method. Genuine silver pieces are required to be hallmarked in most countries. Find "925", "STERLING", "999", or "800" to confirm silver content instantly.
  2. The Magnet Test: Silver is not magnetic. Hold a strong neodymium magnet near the item. If it's attracted, the item is not solid silver (though this doesn't rule out plating). If it has no reaction, it passes — but this isn't conclusive alone.
  3. The Ice Test: Silver has the highest thermal conductivity of any element. Place an ice cube on the silver item — it should melt noticeably faster than on a glass surface or other metal. Not definitive but a fun quick check.
  4. The Bleach Test: Apply one drop of household bleach to a hidden spot. Genuine silver will immediately tarnish and darken. Rinse it off quickly. Non-silver items or plated items won't react the same way. Use with caution — bleach can damage finishes.
  5. Acid Testing (Most Reliable DIY): Silver testing acid kits (available at jeweler supply stores for $10–20) apply nitric acid to a scratch on a test stone. The color of the reaction indicates the purity level. 925 sterling produces a cream/off-white reaction; fine silver produces a brighter color. Follow the kit instructions carefully and use gloves.
  6. XRF Analysis (Gold Standard): X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) guns used by professional refiners and dealers give an instant, precise, non-destructive readout of exact metal composition. Many dealers offer free testing. This is the only way to be 100% certain of purity without melting the item.

Bottom line: For anything with a clear hallmark from a known maker or government mint, you can trust it. For unmarked, foreign, or suspicious items — acid test or visit a local refiner before assuming value.

6. Tips for Selling Scrap Silver — Online vs Local Buyers

You've calculated your silver's melt value. Now the question is: who will pay the most? The answer depends on your quantity, location, and how quickly you need the money. Here's a full breakdown of every option.

Online Silver Refiners

  • ✅ Typically pay 90–98% of melt value
  • ✅ Accept all purities and quantities
  • ✅ Free shipping kits usually provided
  • ❌ Takes 1–3 weeks to receive payment
  • ❌ Must ship your silver (some risk)
  • ❌ Minimum quantities often required

Local Coin Dealers

  • ✅ Immediate cash payment
  • ✅ No shipping required
  • ✅ Can negotiate in person
  • ❌ Typically pay 70–85% of melt value
  • ❌ May not accept all types of silver
  • ❌ Hours/location may be inconvenient

Pawn Shops

  • ✅ Fast, same-day cash
  • ✅ No minimum quantity
  • ❌ Usually only offer 50–70% of melt value
  • ❌ May use incorrect scales or methods
  • ❌ Not ideal for large quantities

eBay / Peer-to-Peer

  • ✅ Can fetch above melt value for collectibles
  • ✅ Sterling flatware sets sell well
  • ❌ Fees eat 10–15% of the sale price
  • ❌ Time-consuming to list and ship
  • ❌ Risk of disputes / scam buyers

Before You Sell: 5 Key Tips

  1. Get multiple quotes. Always visit or request quotes from at least 3 buyers. Prices vary widely — we've seen the same silver quoted at 65% and 92% of melt value at different dealers.
  2. Know your melt value first. Use our calculator before you walk in the door. Buyers who know you understand the value will often offer more.
  3. Separate your silver by purity. Don't mix 999 fine with 925 sterling — dealers may pay you at the lower rate for the entire lot if everything is mixed together.
  4. Weigh it yourself. Bring your own scale or verify the dealer's scale with a reference weight. Scale manipulation is unfortunately not unheard of.
  5. Watch the timing. The gold-to-silver ratio shown in our calculator is a useful indicator. When the ratio exceeds 80, silver is historically undervalued — it may be worth waiting. When it's below 60, silver is relatively expensive — a potentially good time to sell.
